Strontium - Element Details

Symbol: Sr

Atomic Number: 38

Atomic Mass: 87.62 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 5s²

Group: 2 (Alkaline Earth Metals)

Period: 5

Block: s-block

Category: Alkaline Earth Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, highly reactive

Density: ~2.64 g/cm³

Melting Point: 777 °C

Boiling Point: 1377 °C

Discovery

Strontium was discovered by Scottish chemist and physician Adair Crawford in 1790, who identified it in a mineral from Strontian, Scotland. The element was first isolated by Sir Humphry Davy in 1808 through the electrolysis of molten strontium chloride.

Abundance & Sources

Strontium is the 15th most abundant element in the Earth's crust. It is found in nature almost exclusively in mineral forms, with the most important being strontianite (SrCO₃) and celestine (SrSO₄).

Industrial Preparation

Strontium is primarily produced from celestine and strontianite ores. The most common method involves converting these minerals into strontium carbonate, which can then be reduced by aluminum in a vacuum furnace to yield metallic strontium.

Uses

  • Used to produce the brilliant red color in fireworks and flares (strontium salts like strontium nitrate).
  • Strontium carbonate is used in the manufacturing of cathode ray tubes (CRTs) for color televisions to block X-ray emissions (though less common now with flat screens).
  • Strontium aluminate is used in "glow-in-the-dark" pigments.
  • Strontium-90, a radioactive isotope, has been used as a power source in radioisotope thermoelectric generators (RTGs) for remote applications, though its use is debated due to its radioactivity.
  • Strontium ranelate is a medication used in the treatment of osteoporosis.

Interesting Facts

  • Strontium metal is highly reactive and readily oxidizes in air, forming a dark oxide layer.
  • It reacts vigorously with water, similar to calcium, producing hydrogen gas and strontium hydroxide.
  • Its characteristic crimson red flame color is used as a qualitative test for the element.

Did You Know?

While many alkaline earth metals play a role in biological processes, strontium is not considered essential for humans. However, its chemical similarity to calcium means it can be incorporated into bones, which is why radioactive strontium-90 is a particular concern as a fallout product.
