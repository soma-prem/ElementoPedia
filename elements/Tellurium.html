Tellurium - Element Details

Symbol: Te

Atomic Number: 52

Atomic Mass: 127.60 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d¹⁰ 5s² 5p⁴

Group: 16 (Chalcogens)

Period: 5

Block: p-block

Category: Semimetal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, lustrous

Density: ~6.24 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~449.5 °C

Boiling Point: ~988 °C

Discovery

Tellurium was discovered in 1782 by Hungarian scientist Franz-Joseph Müller von Reichenstein while he was investigating gold ore from a mine in what is now Romania. He initially suspected it was a new element but did not prove it. In 1798, German chemist Martin Heinrich Klaproth isolated the element and gave it the name "tellurium" after the Latin word "tellus," meaning "earth."

Abundance & Sources

Tellurium is a very rare element in the Earth's crust, even rarer than gold. It is not usually found in its native form but occurs in telluride minerals, such as calaverite ($AuTe_2$) and sylvanite (($Ag,Au)Te_2$). The majority of tellurium is produced as a byproduct of copper and lead refining.

Industrial Preparation

The industrial production of tellurium is almost entirely a byproduct of the electrolytic refining of copper. The anode slimes produced during this process are treated with chemicals to dissolve the tellurium, which is then precipitated as telluric acid. The telluric acid is subsequently heated and reduced with carbon to yield pure tellurium metal.

Uses

  • Tellurium is used to improve the machinability of copper and stainless steel alloys.
  • In the form of cadmium telluride (CdTe), it is a crucial component in thin-film solar cells.
  • It is used in certain types of rewritable optical media, such as CDs and DVDs, due to its ability to change its electrical resistance when heated.
  • Tellurium is used as a vulcanizing agent for rubber and as a catalyst in various chemical processes.
  • In some thermoelectric devices, tellurium compounds are used to convert heat directly into electricity.

Interesting Facts

  • Tellurium is a metalloid, having properties of both metals and nonmetals.
  • The atomic mass of tellurium (127.60) is higher than that of iodine (126.90), the element that immediately follows it in the periodic table. This is one of the few exceptions to the general rule of increasing atomic mass with increasing atomic number.
  • Exposure to tellurium or its compounds can cause "tellurium breath," a garlicky odor in the breath, sweat, and urine.

Did You Know?

The name for the element "tellurium" comes from the Latin word for Earth, "tellus," and the element "selenium" (its close cousin) comes from the Greek word for Moon, "selene."
