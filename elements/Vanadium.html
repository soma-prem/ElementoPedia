Vanadium - Element Details

Symbol: V

Atomic Number: 23

Atomic Mass: 50.9415 u

Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d³ 4s²

Group: 5 (Transition Metals)

Period: 4

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-grey metal

Density: ~6.11 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1910 °C

Boiling Point: ~3407 °C

Discovery

Vanadium was first discovered by Spanish mineralogist Andrés Manuel del Río in 1801, who named it "erythronium". However, it was later rediscovered by Swedish chemist Nils Gabriel Sefström in 1830, who named it "vanadium" after Vanadís, the Old Norse name for the Scandinavian goddess Freyja, due to its beautiful array of colors in compounds.

Abundance & Sources

Vanadium is the 20th most abundant element in the Earth's crust. It is found in about 65 different minerals, including patronite, vanadinite, and carnotite. Most vanadium is produced from steel slags and as a byproduct of uranium mining.

Industrial Preparation

Vanadium is typically extracted from its ores by roasting with sodium chloride or sodium carbonate to produce sodium vanadate, which is then further processed to obtain vanadium pentoxide (V₂O₅). The pure metal can be obtained by reducing V₂O₅ with calcium or aluminum.

Uses

  • Used primarily in alloys with steel to produce strong, tough, and shock-resistant high-strength low-alloy (HSLA) steels, often used in tools, surgical instruments, and engine parts.
  • Vanadium pentoxide is used as a catalyst in the production of sulfuric acid (contact process).
  • Vanadium is also used in redox flow batteries for large-scale energy storage.
  • Some vanadium compounds are used as pigments in ceramics and glass.

Interesting Facts

  • Vanadium is highly resistant to corrosion from alkalis, sulfuric acid, and hydrochloric acid.
  • It can form compounds with many different oxidation states, leading to a wide range of vibrant colors in its solutions.
  • Some marine organisms, like sea squirts, accumulate vanadium in high concentrations.

Did You Know?

The addition of small amounts of vanadium to steel can significantly increase its strength and prevent grain growth at high temperatures, making it invaluable for applications requiring both strength and heat resistance.
