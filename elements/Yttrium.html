Yttrium - Element Details

Symbol: Y

Atomic Number: 39

Atomic Mass: 88.90584 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d¹ 5s²

Group: 3 (Transition Metals)

Period: 5

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal, Rare-earth element

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white metal

Density: ~4.47 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1526 °C

Boiling Point: ~2930 °C

Discovery

Yttrium was discovered in 1787 by Swedish military officer and part-time chemist Carl Axel Arrhenius, who found a new black mineral in a quarry near the village of Ytterby, Sweden. It was later isolated as an element in 1828 by German chemist Friedrich Wöhler. The element and the mineral were named after the village.

Abundance & Sources

Yttrium is found in almost all rare-earth minerals and is never found as a free element in nature. It is typically a component of minerals such as gadolinite, xenotime, and yttrofluorite.

Industrial Preparation

Yttrium is separated from other rare-earth elements by ion-exchange or solvent extraction methods. The pure metal is produced by reducing yttrium fluoride ($YF_3$) with calcium metal.

Uses

  • Used in the production of LEDs and phosphors, particularly for producing the red color in older cathode ray tube (CRT) television screens.
  • Used as a catalyst in catalytic converters to reduce automotive emissions.
  • Yttrium-stabilized zirconia (YSZ) is used in high-temperature applications, such as thermal barrier coatings and solid oxide fuel cells.
  • Used in specialized lasers, electrodes, and superconductors.
  • The radioisotope Yttrium-90 (Y-90) is used in cancer treatment (radioembolization).

Interesting Facts

  • Yttrium is chemically similar to the lanthanides and is often classified as a rare-earth element.
  • It has no known biological role in humans and is considered non-toxic.
  • The village of Ytterby, Sweden, is unique for having four elements named after it: Yttrium (Y), Terbium (Tb), Erbium (Er), and Ytterbium (Yb).

Did You Know?

The discovery of yttrium led to the eventual discovery of many other elements in the lanthanide series, as yttrium-containing minerals were found to be complex mixtures of these new rare-earth elements.
