Zirconium - Element Details

Symbol: Zr

Atomic Number: 40

Atomic Mass: 91.224 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d² 5s²

Group: 4 (Transition Metals)

Period: 5

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-grey metal

Density: ~6.511 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1855 °C

Boiling Point: ~4409 °C

Discovery

Zirconium was identified in 1789 by German chemist Martin Heinrich Klaproth in a mineral called zircon. The pure metallic form of zirconium was not isolated until 1824 by Swedish chemist Jöns Jacob Berzelius.

Abundance & Sources

Zirconium is the 18th most abundant element in the Earth's crust. It is primarily extracted from the mineral zircon ($ZrSiO_4$), which is found in coastal sands in countries like Australia, South Africa, and Indonesia.

Industrial Preparation

The most common method for producing zirconium metal is the Kroll process. This involves heating zircon to produce zirconium dioxide ($ZrO_2$), which is then reacted with chlorine gas and carbon to form zirconium tetrachloride ($ZrCl_4$). The $ZrCl_4$ is then reduced with magnesium to produce zirconium metal.

Uses

  • Used in nuclear power plants as a cladding for nuclear fuel rods due to its low neutron absorption cross-section and high corrosion resistance.
  • Zirconium alloys are used in the chemical industry for piping and heat exchangers because of their exceptional resistance to acids and alkalis.
  • Zirconium dioxide (zirconia) is used in high-end ceramics, abrasives, and as a durable, biocompatible material for dental and surgical implants.
  • Cubic zirconia is a synthetic gemstone used as a diamond substitute in jewelry.

Interesting Facts

  • Zirconium is highly resistant to corrosion from acids, alkalis, and seawater.
  • It is non-toxic and biocompatible, making it suitable for medical implants.
  • The name "zirconium" comes from the mineral zircon, which in turn comes from the Persian word "zargun," meaning "gold-colored."

Did You Know?

Up to 90% of all zirconium produced globally is used in the nuclear power industry, primarily for constructing the fuel rods in nuclear reactors. This is due to its unique combination of high melting point, corrosion resistance, and neutron transparency.
