Actinium - Element Details

Symbol: Ac

Atomic Number: 89

Atomic Mass: [227] u

Electron Configuration: [Rn] 6d¹ 7s²

Group: 3 (Actinides)

Period: 7

Block: d-block

Category: Actinide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white metal

Density: ~10.07 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1050 °C

Boiling Point: ~3200 °C

Discovery

Actinium was discovered in 1899 by French chemist **André-Louis Debierne** while he was working with the Curies on a residue from pitchblende, the same ore from which they discovered polonium and radium. He named it "actinium" from the Greek word *aktinos*, meaning "**ray**" or "**beam**," because of its intense radioactivity. It was also independently discovered in 1902 by Friedrich Oskar Giesel, who named it "emanium," but Debierne's name was officially adopted.

Abundance & Sources

Actinium is an extremely rare, radioactive element. It is a decay product of uranium-235, so it is found in minute amounts in all uranium ores. It is estimated that one ton of uranium ore contains only about one-fifth of a gram of actinium. It is much more practical to produce actinium synthetically.

Industrial Preparation

Due to its rarity and radioactivity, actinium is not produced commercially on a large scale. It is typically synthesized in a nuclear reactor by bombarding the isotope **radium-226** with neutrons. This process is used to produce small amounts of actinium for scientific and medical research.

Uses

  • Actinium has no significant commercial applications. Its primary use is in **scientific research**, where it is used as a powerful source of alpha particles.
  • The radioisotope **Actinium-225** is currently being researched for use in **targeted alpha therapy**, a new form of cancer treatment that uses the potent alpha radiation to destroy cancer cells with minimal damage to surrounding healthy tissue.
  • It can also be used as a neutron source in certain specialized laboratory settings.

Interesting Facts

  • Actinium is a soft, silvery-white metal that is extremely radioactive. It is about **150 times more radioactive than radium**.
  • Due to its intense radioactivity, actinium glows faintly in the dark with a pale blue light, which is produced by the ionization of the surrounding air by its emitted particles.
  • Actinium is the first element of the actinide series, a group of 15 elements with similar chemical properties, which is named after it.

Did You Know?

The intense radioactivity of actinium makes it a powerful source of alpha particles, which can be used to produce neutrons. In equilibrium with its decay products, a sample of actinium can be a more powerful source of radiation than a similar amount of radium.
