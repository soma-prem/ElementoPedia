Americium - Element Details

Symbol: Am

Atomic Number: 95

Atomic Mass: [243] u

Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f⁷ 7s²

Group: 3 (Actinides)

Period: 7

Block: f-block

Category: Actinide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, tarnishes slowly in dry air

Density: ~13.67 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1176 °C

Boiling Point: ~2011 °C

Discovery

Americium was discovered in 1944 by a team of scientists led by **Glenn T. Seaborg** at the University of Chicago. It was one of the first transuranic elements to be synthesized. They produced it by bombarding plutonium with neutrons. The new element was named in honor of the **Americas**, in analogy to europium which was named after the continent of Europe.

Abundance & Sources

Americium is a synthetic element and does not exist in nature in any significant quantities. It is produced in nuclear reactors as a byproduct of the nuclear fuel cycle.

Industrial Preparation

Americium is produced by bombarding plutonium with neutrons in a nuclear reactor. The resulting heavier isotopes then undergo a series of radioactive decays to form americium. The americium is then chemically separated and purified from the other elements.

Uses

  • The most common and widespread use of americium is in **household smoke detectors**. The isotope **Americium-241** is the active component.
  • It is also used as a portable gamma ray source for industrial gauges, such as those used to measure the thickness of glass, paper, and metal sheets.
  • Americium is used as a source of neutrons for well logging applications in the oil and gas industry.

Interesting Facts

  • Americium is a highly radioactive and toxic heavy metal. Its most common isotope, Am-241, has a half-life of 432 years.
  • It is one of the very few synthetic elements with a common household use.
  • Americium is a dense, silvery-white metal that tarnishes slowly in dry air.

Did You Know?

A tiny amount of Americium-241, often less than 1 microgram, is the key component inside most household smoke detectors. It emits alpha particles that ionize the air in a chamber, allowing a small electric current to flow. When smoke enters the chamber, it disrupts this current, triggering the alarm.
