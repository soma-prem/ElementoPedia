Antimony - Element Details

Symbol: Sb

Atomic Number: 51

Atomic Mass: 121.760 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d¹⁰ 5s² 5p³

Group: 15 (Pnictogens)

Period: 5

Block: p-block

Category: Semimetal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, lustrous metal-like appearance

Density: ~6.697 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~630.63 °C

Boiling Point: ~1587 °C

Discovery

Antimony has been known since ancient times. Its compounds were used in cosmetics, medicine, and as an alloying agent. The first detailed description of the preparation of antimony metal was published in 1540 by the German metallurgist Vannoccio Biringuccio, but the element was likely known much earlier. The name "antimony" is thought to derive from the Greek words "anti" and "monos," meaning "not alone," referring to its presence in various compounds. Its symbol, Sb, comes from the Latin word "stibium."

Abundance & Sources

Antimony is not a common element in the Earth's crust. It is primarily found in the mineral stibnite ($Sb_2S_3$), which is the chief source of the element. The largest reserves and producers are China, Russia, and Bolivia.

Industrial Preparation

Antimony is typically extracted from stibnite by heating the ore in a furnace to drive off the sulfur. The antimony sulfide is then roasted in air to convert it to antimony oxide ($Sb_2O_3$), which is then reduced with carbon in a reverberatory furnace to produce the metal. Alternatively, the antimony sulfide can be directly reduced with iron scrap.

Uses

  • The most important use of antimony is as a hardening alloy for lead, particularly in lead-acid batteries for vehicles.
  • Antimony compounds are used as flame retardants in plastics, textiles, and other materials.
  • It is a component in certain solders and low-friction alloys (babbitt metal) used in bearings.
  • Antimony sulfide is used as a pigment in paints and plastics and in the tips of "strike-anywhere" matches.
  • In the semiconductor industry, antimony is used as a dopant for silicon to create n-type semiconductors.

Interesting Facts

  • Antimony is a brittle, silvery-white semimetal with a layered crystal structure.
  • It has multiple allotropes, with the metallic form being the most stable.
  • Historically, antimony sulfide was used as kohl (a type of eyeliner) in ancient Egypt.
  • Antimony is a toxic element, and its compounds can cause health issues if ingested or inhaled.

Did You Know?

The Roman naturalist Pliny the Elder referred to antimony sulfide as "stibium," which is where the element's chemical symbol, Sb, originates.
