Arsenic - Element Details

Symbol: As

Atomic Number: 33

Atomic Mass: 74.921595 u

Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s² 4p³

Group: 15 (Pnictogens)

Period: 4

Block: p-block

Category: Metalloid

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Metallic gray (most common allotrope)

Density: ~5.727 g/cm³

Melting Point: 817 °C (under high pressure, sublimes at 615 °C)

Boiling Point: 615 °C (sublimes)

Discovery

Arsenic has been known since ancient times in various compounds. The element itself is attributed to Albertus Magnus (Albert the Great) in the 13th century, who described its isolation.

Abundance & Sources

Arsenic is found in the Earth's crust, often in combination with sulfur and various metals, in minerals such as arsenopyrite (FeAsS), realgar (As₄S₂), and orpiment (As₂S₃). It is also found as a byproduct in the smelting of copper, lead, and gold ores.

Industrial Preparation

Arsenic is typically obtained as a byproduct of non-ferrous metal smelting. Arsenopyrite, for example, is heated, causing arsenic to sublime and condense as a solid. Further purification can be achieved through distillation.

Uses

  • Historically used in pesticides, herbicides, and wood preservatives (e.g., chromated copper arsenate), though these uses have been largely phased out due to toxicity concerns.
  • Used in semiconductors, particularly in gallium arsenide (GaAs) for high-speed electronic devices, infrared devices, and optoelectronics.
  • In small quantities, used to harden lead alloys for car batteries and ammunition.
  • Certain arsenic compounds are used in specialized glass production.
  • Arsenic trioxide is used in some medical treatments, particularly for certain types of leukemia.

Interesting Facts

  • Arsenic is famously known for its toxicity, and it has been used as a poison throughout history.
  • It exists in several allotropic forms, with metallic grey arsenic being the most stable.
  • When heated in air, arsenic quickly oxidizes, emitting a garlic-like odor.

Did You Know?

Despite its notorious toxicity, arsenic compounds have medicinal applications. For example, arsenic trioxide is used in chemotherapy for acute promyelocytic leukemia, demonstrating the complex nature of elements and their biological interactions.
