Astatine - Element Details

Symbol: At

Atomic Number: 85

Atomic Mass: [210] u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s² 6p⁵

Group: 17 (Halogens)

Period: 6

Block: p-block

Category: Nonmetal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Unknown, likely dark or metallic

Density: ~6.35 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~302 °C

Boiling Point: ~337 °C

Discovery

Astatine was discovered in 1940 by American physicists **Dale R. Corson**, **Kenneth Ross MacKenzie**, and **Emilio Segrè**. They synthesized it by bombarding bismuth-209 with alpha particles (helium nuclei) at the University of California, Berkeley's cyclotron. They named it "astatine" from the Greek word *astatos*, meaning "**unstable**," because of its very short half-life and extreme radioactivity.

Abundance & Sources

Astatine is the **rarest naturally occurring element** in the Earth's crust. It is estimated that there is less than a single gram of astatine present on Earth at any given time. It is a decay product of uranium and thorium. For all practical purposes, astatine must be produced artificially in particle accelerators.

Industrial Preparation

Due to its scarcity and short half-life, astatine is only produced in research laboratories and for medical applications. The process involves bombarding a target of bismuth-209 with energetic alpha particles in a particle accelerator. The resulting astatine is then purified through distillation. Only minute amounts of the element can be produced at a time.

Uses

  • The radioisotope **Astatine-211** is used in **targeted alpha particle therapy** for cancer treatment. Its potent alpha radiation and short half-life make it a highly effective and precise tool for destroying cancer cells with minimal damage to surrounding healthy tissue.
  • Astatine is also used in a variety of scientific research applications, particularly in radiochemistry and nuclear medicine.

Interesting Facts

  • Astatine is so rare and unstable that its bulk physical properties are largely unknown. Many of its properties, such as its color and melting point, are estimated based on its position in the periodic table as a halogen.
  • Its most stable isotope, Astatine-210, has a half-life of only 8.1 hours.
  • As a halogen, it is expected to have some non-metallic properties, but due to its high atomic mass, it is also believed to exhibit some metallic characteristics.

Did You Know?

If you could magically gather all the astatine that exists naturally on Earth at a single moment, it would be so small an amount that it would likely fit inside a cube no larger than a few millimeters on each side.
