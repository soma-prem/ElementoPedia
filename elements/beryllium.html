Beryllium - Element Details

Symbol: Be

Atomic Number: 4

Atomic Mass: 9.0122 u

Electron Configuration: 1s2 2s2

Group: 2 (Alkaline Earth Metals)

Period: 2

Block: s-block

Category: Alkaline Earth Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Steel gray

Odor: Odorless

Melting Point: 1287 °C

Boiling Point: 2469 °C

Discovery

Beryllium was discovered in 1798 by French chemist Louis Nicolas Vauquelin in beryl and emeralds. It was later isolated as a metal by Friedrich Wöhler and Antoine Bussy in 1828.

Abundance & Sources

Beryllium is relatively rare in Earth's crust. It is mainly obtained from minerals such as beryl and bertrandite. The United States and China are leading producers of beryllium.

Industrial Preparation

Beryllium is typically extracted from bertrandite or beryl ore through a combination of chemical and thermal processing. Pure beryllium is isolated by reducing beryllium fluoride with magnesium.

Uses

  • Used in aerospace materials due to its lightweight and stiffness
  • Used in X-ray windows due to transparency to X-rays
  • Used in nuclear reactors as a moderator and reflector
  • Added to copper and other alloys to improve hardness

Interesting Facts

  • Beryllium is highly toxic if inhaled as dust or fumes
  • It has one of the highest melting points among light metals
  • It has a high stiffness-to-weight ratio
  • Its name is derived from the mineral "beryl"

Did You Know?

Beryllium is used in the James Webb Space Telescope's mirrors because it can withstand the extreme cold of space while maintaining its shape and performance.
