Bismuth - Element Details

Symbol: Bi

Atomic Number: 83

Atomic Mass: 208.98040 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s² 6p³

Group: 15 (Post-transition Metals)

Period: 6

Block: p-block

Category: Poor Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-pinkish, crystalline, brittle metal

Density: ~9.78 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~271.4 °C

Boiling Point: ~1564 °C

Discovery

Bismuth has been known since ancient times, but it was often confused with lead and tin due to its similar physical properties. It was first formally recognized as a distinct element in 1753 by the French chemist **Claude-François Geoffroy**, who demonstrated that it was different from both lead and tin.

Abundance & Sources

Bismuth is a relatively rare metal in the Earth's crust. It is found both in its native, uncombined state and in minerals such as bismuthinite ($Bi_2S_3$) and bismite ($Bi_2O_3$). The majority of the world's bismuth is not mined directly but is obtained as a byproduct of refining the ores of other metals, particularly lead, copper, and tin. The main producing countries are China, Mexico, and Peru.

Industrial Preparation

Since bismuth is a byproduct of other metal refining, its industrial preparation involves a number of complex recovery and purification processes. It is typically recovered from the anode mud generated during the electrolytic refining of lead and copper. The crude bismuth is then refined to high purity through chemical methods.

Uses

  • The primary use of bismuth is as a component in **low-melting-point alloys** that are often used as a replacement for toxic lead in solders, plumbing, and fire sprinklers.
  • **Bismuth subsalicylate** is the active ingredient in many common stomach remedies, such as Pepto-Bismol.
  • Bismuth compounds are used in **cosmetics**, particularly in eye shadows and nail polishes, where they provide a pearlescent sheen.
  • It is used in some catalysts for the production of synthetic fibers and rubber.
  • Bismuth is also being investigated as a potential component in nuclear fuel and as a non-toxic alternative to lead in shotgun pellets.

Interesting Facts

  • Bismuth is the **heaviest non-radioactive element**, which is a distinction it has held for a long time. However, its most common isotope, Bismuth-209, is now known to be radioactive with an extremely long half-life (longer than the age of the universe), so it is considered stable for all practical purposes.
  • When melted bismuth cools and solidifies, it forms stunning, iridescent, hopper-shaped crystals. This unique property makes it a popular material for hobbyists and collectors.
  • Bismuth has a very low thermal conductivity and is a strong diamagnetic material.

Did You Know?

Bismuth is one of the few elements that **expands as it solidifies**, a property it shares with water. This makes it useful in some alloys for producing very sharp castings with intricate details.
