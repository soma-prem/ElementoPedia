Bohrium - Element Details

Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f¹⁴ 6d⁵ 7s²

Group: 7 (Transition Metals)

Period: 7

Category: Transition Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Unknown, likely silvery-white or gray

Density: ~37.1 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~2900 °C

Boiling Point: ~- °C

Discovery

Bohrium was discovered in 1981 by a German research team led by **Peter Armbruster** and **Gottfried Münzenberg** at the GSI Helmholtz Centre for Heavy Ion Research in Darmstadt. They synthesized it by bombarding a bismuth-209 target with chromium-54 ions. The element was named in honor of the Danish physicist **Niels Bohr**, a pioneer of atomic structure theory.

Abundance & Sources

Bohrium is a synthetic, radioactive element that does not exist in nature. It is produced in laboratories in minute quantities for scientific research.

Industrial Preparation

Bohrium is produced by nuclear fusion reactions in a particle accelerator. A target of Bismuth-209 is bombarded with a beam of Chromium-54 ions, causing the nuclei to fuse and create a new, heavier element.

Uses

  • Bohrium has **no commercial applications** due to its extreme scarcity, high radioactivity, and very short half-life.
  • Its sole use is in **scientific research** to further understand the fundamental properties of the heaviest elements.

Interesting Facts

  • Bohrium is a highly radioactive element with a very short half-life. The most stable isotope, Bohrium-270, has a half-life of only about 61 seconds.
  • It is named after a person who was not involved in its discovery, which was a departure from previous naming conventions.
  • Bohrium is predicted to have chemical properties similar to its lighter homologues in Group 7, such as rhenium and manganese.

Did You Know?

Due to the short half-life of its isotopes, experiments involving bohrium must be performed quickly and on a single-atom basis. This makes the study of its chemical properties a remarkable feat of experimental science.
