Bromine - Element Details

Symbol: Br

Atomic Number: 35

Atomic Mass: 79.904 u

Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s² 4p⁵

Group: 17 (Halogens)

Period: 4

Block: p-block

Category: Nonmetal

State at Room Temperature: Liquid

Color: Reddish-brown

Odor: Pungent, bleach-like

Density: ~3.1028 g/cm³

Melting Point: -7.2 °C

Boiling Point: 58.8 °C

Discovery

Bromine was independently discovered by two chemists in 1826: Antoine Jérôme Balard in France and Carl Jacob Löwig in Germany. Balard published his findings first and named it bromine, from the Greek word "bromos," meaning "stench."

Abundance & Sources

Bromine is relatively rare in the Earth's crust, but it is found in significant quantities in bromide salts dissolved in seawater, salt lakes (like the Dead Sea), and underground brine deposits.

Industrial Preparation

Commercially, bromine is extracted from brines rich in bromide ions. The primary method involves oxidation of bromide ions ($Br^-$) with chlorine gas ($Cl_2$) to produce elemental bromine ($Br_2$), which is then purified by distillation.

Uses

  • Used in fire retardants for plastics and textiles.
  • In agriculture as a fumigant for pest control (e.g., methyl bromide, though usage is declining).
  • Historically used in photography as silver bromide (AgBr) in photographic films and papers.
  • Bromine compounds are used in water purification and as disinfectants.
  • Used in some specialized drilling fluids.

Interesting Facts

  • Bromine is the only nonmetallic element that is liquid at room temperature.
  • It is highly corrosive and toxic, especially in its vapor form.
  • Bromine has a distinctive, strong, and unpleasant odor.
  • It reacts readily with many elements and organic compounds.

Did You Know?

The intense red-orange color of liquid bromine and its reddish-brown vapor make it one of the most visually striking elements on the periodic table. Its name, meaning "stench," aptly describes its strong and disagreeable odor.
