Cadmium - Element Details

Symbol: Cd

Atomic Number: 48

Atomic Mass: 112.414 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d¹⁰ 5s²

Group: 12 (Transition Metals)

Period: 5

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white metal with a bluish tinge

Density: ~8.65 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~321.07 °C

Boiling Point: ~767 °C

Discovery

Cadmium was discovered in 1817 by German chemist Friedrich Stromeyer. He was examining zinc carbonate for its purity when he found an impurity that was a new element. He named it after the Latin word "cadmia" and the Greek word "kadmeia," both ancient names for calamine (zinc carbonate).

Abundance & Sources

Cadmium is a relatively rare element in the Earth's crust. It is typically found as a minor component in zinc ores, particularly sphalerite. As a result, almost all commercially produced cadmium is obtained as a byproduct of zinc refining.

Industrial Preparation

The industrial preparation of cadmium is closely linked to zinc production. During the roasting of zinc sulfide ores, cadmium, being more volatile than zinc, is concentrated in the flue dust. This dust is then treated with various chemical processes to isolate and purify the cadmium metal.

Uses

  • Historically, a major use of cadmium was in rechargeable nickel-cadmium (Ni-Cd) batteries, but this is declining due to its toxicity.
  • Cadmium compounds were used to create bright yellow, orange, and red pigments for paints and plastics, often known as "cadmium yellow" or "cadmium red."
  • It has been used in electroplating to provide a protective, corrosion-resistant coating for steel and other metals.
  • Cadmium is a key component in cadmium telluride (CdTe) solar panels, which are an important type of thin-film solar technology.
  • Due to its ability to absorb neutrons, it is used in control rods in some nuclear reactors.

Interesting Facts

  • When a bar of pure cadmium is bent, it emits a crackling sound, similar to that of tin.
  • Cadmium and its compounds are highly toxic and exposure can cause serious health problems, including cancer and kidney damage.
  • The use of cadmium in many products is being phased out in many parts of the world due to its environmental and health risks.

Did You Know?

The famous "Itai-Itai" disease in Japan was caused by chronic cadmium poisoning from industrial pollution that contaminated rice fields. The disease led to severe bone pain and fractures.
