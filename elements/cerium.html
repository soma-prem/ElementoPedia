Cerium - Element Details

Symbol: Ce

Atomic Number: 58

Atomic Mass: 140.116 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f¹ 5d¹ 6s²

Group: 3 (Lanthanides)

Period: 6

Block: f-block

Category: Lanthanide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, ductile metal

Density: ~6.77 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~795 °C

Boiling Point: ~3443 °C

Discovery

Cerium was discovered in 1803 by German chemist Martin Heinrich Klaproth and, independently, by Swedish chemists Jöns Jacob Berzelius and Wilhelm Hisinger. Klaproth named the new element after the dwarf planet Ceres, which had been discovered just two years earlier. The new element was found in the mineral cerite, which was later named after the element itself.

Abundance & Sources

Cerium is the most abundant of the rare-earth elements, making up about 0.0046% of the Earth's crust. It is never found in its pure form in nature but is a component of many rare-earth minerals, primarily bastnäsite and monazite. Major sources of these minerals include China, the United States, and Australia.

Industrial Preparation

Cerium is extracted from its ores, typically as a byproduct of lanthanum and neodymium production. The process involves treating the crushed ore with acids, followed by complex separation techniques such as solvent extraction and ion-exchange to isolate the cerium compounds. The pure metal is then produced by the electrolysis of molten cerous chloride.

Uses

  • Cerium is a key component in **catalytic converters for automobiles**, where it helps reduce carbon monoxide (CO) emissions.
  • In the form of mischmetal, an alloy of rare-earth metals, it is used to make **lighter flints** and in metallurgy to remove impurities from steel.
  • Cerium oxide is a highly effective **polishing powder for glass**, used for camera lenses, telescopes, and mirrors.
  • It is used in self-cleaning ovens as a catalyst to help burn off food residue.
  • Cerium is also a component in certain special glasses and alloys, including those used in lighting and aerospace applications.

Interesting Facts

  • Cerium is a highly reactive metal that tarnishes quickly in the air and can spontaneously ignite when scratched or heated to around 150 °C.
  • It is one of the few lanthanides that can exist in more than one stable oxidation state, most commonly +3 and +4.
  • The name "Cerium" is directly linked to the first-ever discovery of a celestial body via a telescope in 1801, the dwarf planet Ceres.

Did You Know?

The use of cerium oxide as a glass polishing agent is a major industrial application. The cerium oxide slurry not only mechanically abrades the glass but also chemically interacts with the surface, resulting in a smoother, higher-quality finish than other polishing compounds.
