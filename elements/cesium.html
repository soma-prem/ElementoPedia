Cesium - Element Details

Symbol: Cs

Atomic Number: 55

Atomic Mass: 132.905 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 6s¹

Group: 1 (Alkali Metals)

Period: 6

Block: s-block

Category: Alkali Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid (Melts just above room temperature)

Color: Silvery-gold metal

Density: ~1.879 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~28.4 °C

Boiling Point: ~671 °C

Discovery

Cesium was discovered in 1860 by German chemists Robert Bunsen and Gustav Kirchhoff. They were the pioneers of spectroscopy and discovered the element by analyzing the spectrum of a mineral water sample from Durkheim, Germany. They observed two bright blue lines and named the element "cesium" from the Latin word "caesius," meaning "sky blue."

Abundance & Sources

Cesium is a relatively rare element. Its main source is the mineral pollucite, found most abundantly in a large deposit at Bernic Lake, Manitoba, Canada. Small amounts are also found in lepidolite and other minerals.

Industrial Preparation

Commercial production of cesium usually involves a process called thermal reduction. Pollucite ore is treated with hydrochloric acid, and the resulting cesium chloride is then separated from other alkali metals. The pure cesium metal is then produced by the reduction of cesium chloride with calcium or a similar metal at high temperatures.

Uses

  • Cesium's most famous use is in atomic clocks, which are the most accurate timekeeping devices in the world.
  • Cesium formate brines are used in the oil and gas industry as a lubricant for high-pressure drilling fluids.
  • Due to its photoelectric properties, it is used in photocells, night vision devices, and other optical equipment.
  • Cesium vapor is used in ion propulsion systems for spacecraft.
  • It is also used in vacuum tubes, as a getter to scavenge residual gases.

Interesting Facts

  • Cesium is the softest of all metals and is so reactive that it will explode on contact with water or even ice.
  • It is one of only five metals that are liquid at or near room temperature.
  • The official definition of a second is based on the frequency of a specific transition of the cesium-133 atom.

Did You Know?

The frequency of light absorbed by a cesium atom (9,192,631,770 hertz) is so precise and stable that it has been used since 1967 to define the length of a second, making cesium atomic clocks the foundation of modern timekeeping.
