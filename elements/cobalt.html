Cobalt - Element Details

Symbol: Co

Atomic Number: 27

Atomic Mass: 58.933194 u

Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d⁷ 4s²

Group: 9 (Transition Metals)

Period: 4

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Lustrous, silver-gray metal with a bluish tint

Density: ~8.90 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1495 °C

Boiling Point: ~2927 °C

Discovery

Cobalt was discovered by Swedish chemist Georg Brandt in 1735. He was able to prove that the blue color in glass, previously attributed to bismuth, was actually due to a new element, which he named cobalt.

Abundance & Sources

Cobalt is a relatively rare element, ranking as the 32nd most abundant in the Earth's crust. It is typically found in minerals such as cobaltite, erythrite, and glaucodot. The Democratic Republic of Congo is the largest producer of cobalt globally.

Industrial Preparation

Cobalt is usually obtained as a byproduct of copper and nickel mining. The extraction process involves roasting the ore to convert sulfides to oxides, followed by leaching and various purification steps, often involving electrolysis or reduction with carbon.

Uses

  • Essential component in lithium-ion batteries, especially for electric vehicles and portable electronics.
  • Used in the production of superalloys for jet engines and gas turbines due to its high-temperature strength and corrosion resistance.
  • Forms strong, permanent magnets (e.g., Alnico magnets).
  • Cobalt-60 is an important radioisotope used in medical treatments (radiotherapy) and sterilization.
  • Cobalt compounds are used as pigments to produce vibrant blue colors in glass, ceramics, and paints.

Interesting Facts

  • The name "cobalt" comes from the German word "Kobold", meaning "goblin" or "evil spirit", given by miners because its ores were difficult to smelt and poisoned other extracted metals.
  • It is one of only three naturally occurring ferromagnetic elements (the others are iron and nickel).
  • Vitamin B12 is the only vitamin that contains cobalt.

Did You Know?

Due to its critical role in lithium-ion batteries and its concentrated supply in certain regions, cobalt has become a strategically important and highly sought-after commodity.
