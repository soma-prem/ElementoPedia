Dysprosium - Element Details

Symbol: Dy

Atomic Number: 66

Atomic Mass: 162.500 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f¹⁰ 6s²

Group: 3 (Lanthanides)

Period: 6

Block: f-block

Category: Lanthanide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, metallic luster

Density: ~8.55 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1407 °C

Boiling Point: ~2567 °C

Discovery

Dysprosium was discovered in 1886 by French chemist **Paul-Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran**. He isolated it from a sample of holmium oxide and was the first to recognize it as a new element. He named it from the Greek word *dysprositos*, meaning "**hard to get**," due to the immense difficulty he had in separating it from the other rare-earth elements.

Abundance & Sources

Dysprosium is a rare-earth element that is not found in its pure form in nature. It is typically found in rare-earth minerals such as monazite and bastnäsite. The primary sources for these minerals are located in China, the United States, and Australia.

Industrial Preparation

The industrial production of dysprosium involves a series of complex separation steps to isolate it from the other rare-earth elements in the ore. The separation is primarily achieved using solvent extraction or ion-exchange chromatography. The pure metal is then produced by the reduction of anhydrous dysprosium fluoride ($DyF_3$) with calcium metal at high temperatures.

Uses

  • Dysprosium is a crucial component in high-performance **neodymium-iron-boron magnets**. Adding dysprosium significantly improves the magnets' coercivity and their ability to function at high temperatures, making them vital for electric vehicle motors and wind turbine generators.
  • It is also a key component in the alloy **Terfenol-D** (Terbium, Dysprosium, Iron), which is used in high-tech sonar systems and other transducers.
  • Dysprosium is used in **control rods for nuclear reactors** because of its high neutron absorption cross-section.
  • It is used in certain types of lasers and as a component in metal halide lamps.

Interesting Facts

  • Dysprosium has one of the highest magnetic moments of any element, a property that makes it invaluable in high-strength magnets.
  • It is a soft, silvery-white metal that can be cut with a knife.
  • Due to its importance in green technologies, dysprosium is considered a strategically important and critical element.

Did You Know?

The high demand for dysprosium in powerful magnets for electric cars and wind turbines has led to it being one of the most expensive and sought-after rare-earth elements on the market.
