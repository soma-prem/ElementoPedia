Europium - Element Details

Symbol: Eu

Atomic Number: 63

Atomic Mass: 151.964 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f⁷ 6s²

Group: 3 (Lanthanides)

Period: 6

Block: f-block

Category: Lanthanide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, malleable metal

Density: ~5.24 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~822 °C

Boiling Point: ~1529 °C

Discovery

Europium was discovered in 1901 by French chemist **Eugène-Anatole Demarçay**. While examining samples of the rare-earth elements samarium and gadolinium, he noticed new spectral lines that indicated the presence of another element. He successfully isolated this new element and named it in honor of the continent of **Europe**.

Abundance & Sources

Europium is one of the rarer lanthanide elements. It is not found in its pure form in nature. The primary commercial sources are the same rare-earth minerals as other lanthanides, such as monazite and bastnäsite. Major deposits are found in China and the United States.

Industrial Preparation

The separation of europium from other rare-earth elements is a key part of its production. Unlike most lanthanides, europium can be easily reduced from its common +3 oxidation state to a more stable +2 state. This unique property allows it to be separated from other elements by precipitation. The pure metal is then obtained by heating europium oxide with lanthanum in a vacuum to high temperatures.

Uses

  • Europium is most famously used as a **phosphor** in television screens and fluorescent lamps to produce the vibrant **red color**.
  • Europium-doped phosphors are also used in some modern LED lighting and display technologies.
  • The unique fluorescent properties of europium compounds are used in **anti-counterfeiting** measures, such as on Euro banknotes and some security inks.
  • Europium is a dopant in some optical glass and has been researched for use in quantum computing.
  • It is also being explored for applications in high-performance solar cells.

Interesting Facts

  • Europium is the most reactive of the lanthanide elements. It oxidizes very quickly when exposed to air.
  • Due to its softness and high reactivity, it is one of the least dense of the rare-earth metals.
  • Europium's unique fluorescence, particularly its bright red color when excited by ultraviolet light, is a defining characteristic and the reason for many of its applications.

Did You Know?

The distinct, bright red color you see on your television screen or computer monitor, especially in older CRT displays, is often a result of europium atoms being excited by electrons or UV light and then emitting a very specific wavelength of red light.
