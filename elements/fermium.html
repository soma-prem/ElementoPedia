Fermium - Element Details

Symbol: Fm

Atomic Number: 100

Atomic Mass: [257] u

Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f¹² 7s²

Group: 3 (Actinides)

Period: 7

Block: f-block

Category: Actinide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white metal

Density: ~9.7 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1527 °C

Boiling Point: ~- °C

Discovery

Fermium was discovered in 1952 by a team of scientists led by **Albert Ghiorso** at the University of California, Berkeley. It was found in the debris of the first **hydrogen bomb test** (Ivy Mike) in the Pacific Ocean. The element was named in honor of **Enrico Fermi**, a pioneer of nuclear physics and the creator of the first nuclear reactor.

Abundance & Sources

Fermium is a synthetic, radioactive element that is not found in nature. It is produced in very small, microscopic quantities in high-flux nuclear reactors.

Industrial Preparation

Fermium is produced in a nuclear reactor by bombarding plutonium or other heavy elements with neutrons. The process involves a series of nuclear reactions and beta decays that eventually yield tiny amounts of fermium isotopes.

Uses

  • Fermium has **no commercial applications** due to its extreme scarcity, high radioactivity, and short half-life.
  • Its primary use is in **scientific research**, particularly in the synthesis of even heavier transuranic elements and in understanding the properties of the actinide series.

Interesting Facts

  • Fermium was the eighth transuranic element to be discovered.
  • The most stable isotope, Fermium-257, has a half-life of about 100.5 days.
  • It is a highly radioactive and toxic element. The half-life of all of its isotopes is too short for it to be produced in macroscopic, usable quantities.

Did You Know?

Fermium-257 is the heaviest element that can be produced by neutron capture, as the next isotope in the process, Fermium-258, is highly unstable and undergoes spontaneous fission almost immediately. This effectively marks a limit to element synthesis via neutron capture.
