Discovery

Flerovium was discovered in 1998 by a Russian-American team at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research (JINR) in Dubna, Russia. The team was led by **Yuri Oganessian**. They synthesized it by bombarding a plutonium-244 target with a calcium-48 beam. The element was named in honor of the physicist **Georgy Flyorov**, who founded the Flerov Laboratory of Nuclear Reactions at JINR.

Abundance & Sources

Flerovium is a synthetic, radioactive element that does not exist in nature. It is produced in laboratories in minute quantities for scientific research.

Industrial Preparation

Flerovium is produced by nuclear fusion reactions in a particle accelerator. The nuclei of Plutonium-244 and Calcium-48 are fused together to create an atom of flerovium.

Uses

  • Flerovium has **no commercial applications** due to its extreme scarcity, high radioactivity, and very short half-life.
  • Its sole use is in **scientific research** to further understand the fundamental properties of the heaviest elements.

Interesting Facts

  • Flerovium is a highly radioactive element with a very short half-life. The most stable isotope, Flerovium-289, has a half-life of only about 2.6 seconds.
  • It is located in the theorized "**island of stability**," which predicts that isotopes with certain numbers of protons and neutrons would have longer half-lives than other superheavy elements.
  • Theoretical predictions suggest that flerovium may be a noble gas, despite its location in the poor metals group, due to relativistic effects.

Did You Know?

The discovery of flerovium was a significant step in the search for the "island of stability." Its relatively longer half-life compared to its neighbors provides tantalizing evidence that this island of stability may actually exist.
