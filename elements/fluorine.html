Fluorine - Element Details

Symbol: F

Atomic Number: 9

Atomic Mass: 18.998 u

Electron Configuration: [He] 2s2 2p5

Group: 17 (Halogens)

Period: 2

Block: p‑block

Category: Halogen (Nonmetal)

State at Room Temperature: Gas

Color: Pale yellow‑green gas

Odor: Pungent

Melting Point: −219.67 °C

Boiling Point: −188.11 °C

Discovery

First discovered in mineral compounds by Georgius Agricola in 16th century and hydrofluoric acid characterized by Scheele in 1771. Successfully isolated in elemental form by French chemist Henri Moissan in 1886 via electrolysis of hydrogen fluoride—earning him the Nobel Prize :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}.

Abundance & Sources

Extremely rare in the universe (≈400 ppb), estimated 24th most common element in solar system :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}. On Earth, found only in mineral form such as fluorite (CaF₂), cryolite, and fluoroapatite :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}.

Uses

  • Manufacture of fluorochemicals (e.g. Teflon, refrigerants like Freon)
  • Water fluoridation to help prevent tooth decay
  • Production of high-end pharmaceuticals—≈20 % contain fluorine :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}
  • Etching glass with hydrofluoric acid
  • Used in uranium enrichment and nuclear fuel processing
  • Fluorine is the most electronegative and reactive element on the periodic table :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}
  • It reacts violently with nearly every other element—including noble gases under specific conditions
  • Forms hydrofluoric acid, a highly corrosive and metabolically dangerous compound
  • Only one stable isotope exists naturally (¹⁹F). Radioisotope ¹⁸F is widely used in PET medical imaging :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}
  • It is used in the production of CFCs and certain pharmaceutical drugs—contributing to ozone depletion and environmental concerns :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

Did You Know?

Despite being highly reactive and rarely found in nature, fluorine compounds such as fluoride are essential for dental health and modern electronics—but elemental fluorine gas itself is one of the most dangerous substances on Earth.
