Francium - Element Details

Symbol: Fr

Atomic Number: 87

Atomic Mass: [223] u

Electron Configuration: [Rn] 7s¹

Group: 1 (Alkali Metals)

Period: 7

Block: s-block

Category: Alkali Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Highly radioactive, metallic (rarely seen)

Density: ~1.87 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~27 °C

Boiling Point: ~677 °C

Discovery

Francium was discovered in 1939 by French chemist **Marguerite Perey** at the Curie Institute in Paris. She found the element as a decay product of actinium-227. Perey named the element "francium" after her home country, **France**.

Abundance & Sources

Francium is one of the **rarest elements** in the Earth's crust. It is estimated that only a few ounces exist naturally at any given time. It occurs naturally in minute amounts in uranium ores as a temporary decay product. For all practical purposes, it is only available as a synthetic product produced in laboratories.

Industrial Preparation

Due to its extreme rarity and short half-life, francium is not produced industrially. It is typically synthesized in nuclear reactions for specialized research purposes in atomic and nuclear physics. Only very small amounts, such as a few hundred thousand atoms, can be made at a time.

Uses

  • Francium has **no commercial uses** due to its extreme rarity and instability.
  • It is primarily used in **scientific research**, particularly in atomic spectroscopy, to study its atomic structure and properties.
  • Francium is also used in biological studies as a tracer to investigate cell functions.

Interesting Facts

  • Francium is the **most unstable of the first 103 elements**, with its most stable isotope, Fr-223, having a half-life of only 22 minutes.
  • It is the **second-most electropositive element** after cesium and is believed to have the lowest electronegativity of all known elements.
  • Because of its extreme radioactivity and short half-life, any macroscopic sample of francium would be vaporized by its own radioactive heat almost instantly.

Did You Know?

Francium is so rare and short-lived that it has never been seen as a bulk liquid or solid. All observations of its properties are based on radio-spectroscopic measurements of very small groups of atoms.
