Gadolinium - Element Details

Symbol: Gd

Atomic Number: 64

Atomic Mass: 157.25 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f⁷ 5d¹ 6s²

Group: 3 (Lanthanides)

Period: 6

Block: f-block

Category: Lanthanide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, lustrous metal

Density: ~7.89 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1312 °C

Boiling Point: ~3273 °C

Discovery

Gadolinium was discovered in 1880 by French chemist **Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac**. He detected the presence of the new element by identifying its unique spectroscopic lines in a sample of the mineral samarskite. He named the new element after the mineral **gadolinite**, which in turn was named in honor of the Finnish chemist **Johan Gadolin**, who had discovered the first rare-earth element, yttrium.

Abundance & Sources

Gadolinium is a moderately abundant rare-earth element, but it is never found in its pure form in nature. The primary commercial sources are the minerals monazite and bastnäsite, with major deposits found in China, the United States, and Australia.

Industrial Preparation

The industrial production of gadolinium involves a series of complex separation steps to isolate it from other rare-earth elements in the ore. The separation is primarily done using solvent extraction and ion-exchange chromatography. The pure metal is then produced by reducing anhydrous gadolinium fluoride ($GdF_3$) with calcium metal at high temperatures.

Uses

  • The most famous use of gadolinium is in **MRI contrast agents**. Its powerful paramagnetic properties enhance the image quality of internal organs and tissues, helping doctors to detect tumors and other abnormalities.
  • Gadolinium has the highest thermal neutron capture cross-section of any known element, making it useful in **control rods** for nuclear reactors and for shielding in neutron radiography.
  • It is used in a special alloy with iron and chromium to improve the workability and high-temperature resistance of these metals.
  • Gadolinium compounds are used as phosphors in color television tubes and in X-ray imaging screens.
  • It is also being explored for use in magnetic refrigeration technology due to the magnetocaloric effect.

Interesting Facts

  • Gadolinium is ferromagnetic at temperatures below 20 °C (room temperature) and is the most paramagnetic element above that temperature.
  • The name Gadolinium is a unique tribute, as it's the only element named after a person who also had a mineral named after them.
  • Despite its name, Johan Gadolin did not discover the element gadolinium itself, but a different rare-earth element in the mineral later named in his honor.

Did You Know?

The use of gadolinium in MRI scans is so effective because of its unique magnetic properties. When injected, it alters the magnetic field of water molecules, causing them to show up much more brightly on the scan and providing a clearer, more detailed image for radiologists.
