Gallium - Element Details

Symbol: Ga

Atomic Number: 31

Atomic Mass: 69.723 u

Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s² 4p¹

Group: 13 (Boron Group)

Period: 4

Block: p-block

Category: Post-transition Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid (but melts just above room temperature)

Color: Silvery-white (solid), silvery (liquid)

Density: ~5.91 g/cm³ (solid), ~6.095 g/cm³ (liquid)

Melting Point: 29.76 °C

Boiling Point: ~2204 °C

Discovery

Gallium was predicted by Dmitri Mendeleev in 1871 and was discovered by French chemist Paul-Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran in 1875 using spectroscopy. He named it after his native land, Gaul (Latin: Gallia).

Abundance & Sources

Gallium is not a common element and does not occur freely in nature. It is typically found in trace amounts in zinc ores (like sphalerite) and bauxite. It is often extracted as a byproduct of aluminum and zinc production.

Industrial Preparation

Gallium is recovered as a byproduct during the refining of bauxite (aluminum ore) and zinc ores. The most common method involves caustic digestion of the bauxite, followed by electrolysis or fractional crystallization to separate gallium from other elements.

Uses

  • Primary use is in gallium arsenide (GaAs) for semiconductors, LEDs, laser diodes, and solar cells due to its superior electronic properties compared to silicon in some applications.
  • Used in high-temperature thermometers because of its wide liquid range.
  • Forms low-melting-point alloys with other metals, which can be used in some medical and dental applications.
  • Gallium nitride (GaN) is used in blue and green LEDs, and in high-power, high-frequency devices.

Interesting Facts

  • Gallium is one of the few metals that can melt in the palm of your hand, as its melting point is just above room temperature.
  • It expands when it freezes, similar to water, which is unusual for metals.
  • Liquid gallium can "wet" glass, meaning it spreads out and sticks to the surface, making it difficult to clean.

Did You Know?

Due to its low melting point and relatively low toxicity, gallium is sometimes used as a non-toxic alternative to mercury in some thermometers and barometers.
