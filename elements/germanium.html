Germanium - Element Details

Symbol: Ge

Atomic Number: 32

Atomic Mass: 72.63 u

Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s² 4p²

Group: 14 (Carbon Group)

Period: 4

Block: p-block

Category: Metalloid

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Greyish-white, lustrous

Density: ~5.323 g/cm³

Melting Point: 938.2 °C

Boiling Point: 2833 °C

Discovery

Germanium was predicted by Dmitri Mendeleev in 1869 (as "eka-silicon") and was discovered by German chemist Clemens Winkler in 1886. Winkler named the element after his home country, Germany.

Abundance & Sources

Germanium is a relatively rare element in the Earth's crust. It is often found in trace amounts in zinc ores, silver ores, and copper ores, and also in some coals. It is typically recovered as a byproduct of zinc refining.

Industrial Preparation

Germanium is primarily obtained as a byproduct of zinc smelting or the combustion of certain coals. It is separated from other elements by fractional distillation of germanium tetrachloride (GeCl₄), which is then hydrolyzed to germanium dioxide (GeO₂), and finally reduced to pure germanium using hydrogen or carbon.

Uses

  • Used extensively in semiconductors for transistors and diodes, especially in early electronics, though largely replaced by silicon.
  • Important for fiber optic communication cables due to its high refractive index.
  • Used in infrared optics, including night vision devices, thermal imaging cameras, and other optical instruments.
  • Used as a polymerization catalyst in the production of certain plastics.
  • Germanium alloys are used in dentistry and for some specific metal applications.

Interesting Facts

  • Germanium is a metalloid, meaning it has properties intermediate between metals and nonmetals.
  • It is an important semiconductor material, allowing for control of electrical conductivity.
  • Early transistors were often made of germanium before silicon became dominant.

Did You Know?

One of the critical uses of germanium is in lenses and windows for infrared optical systems because it is transparent to infrared radiation and has a high refractive index, making it ideal for thermal imaging.
