Hafnium - Element Details

Symbol: Hf

Atomic Number: 72

Atomic Mass: 178.49 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d² 6s²

Group: 4 (Transition Metals)

Period: 6

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-gray, lustrous metal

Density: ~13.31 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~2233 °C

Boiling Point: ~4603 °C

Discovery

Hafnium was discovered in 1923 by Dutch physicist Dirk Coster and Hungarian chemist George de Hevesy in Copenhagen, Denmark. Their discovery was based on Niels Bohr's atomic theory, which predicted an element with an atomic number of 72 would exist and have properties similar to zirconium. They found the element in zirconium-containing ores and named it after *Hafnia*, the Latin name for Copenhagen.

Abundance & Sources

Hafnium is not found in its native form. It is almost always found in zirconium minerals and is a very rare element. Its most common source is the mineral zircon ($ZrSiO_4$), where it is present as a minor impurity. Major sources of zirconium-hafnium minerals include Australia, South Africa, and Brazil.

Industrial Preparation

The production of hafnium is a difficult and expensive process because of its chemical similarity to zirconium. They must be separated before being used in nuclear applications. The separation is most commonly achieved through solvent extraction, and the pure hafnium metal is then produced by the Kroll process, which involves reducing hafnium tetrachloride ($HfCl_4$) with magnesium.

Uses

  • Hafnium is primarily used in **control rods for nuclear reactors** because of its very high capacity to absorb neutrons.
  • Its excellent corrosion resistance and high melting point make it a valuable component in superalloys used in the aerospace industry.
  • Hafnium and its compounds are used as an essential component in the gate oxide insulator of advanced silicon-based integrated circuits.
  • Hafnium carbide ($HfC$) is one of the most refractory binary compounds known and is used in plasma cutting electrodes.
  • It is also used as a getter in some vacuum tubes to scavenge oxygen and nitrogen.

Interesting Facts

  • Hafnium's high neutron-absorption cross-section is the opposite of zirconium's, which is very low. This is why they must be separated for nuclear applications; hafnium is for control rods, and zirconium is for fuel rod cladding.
  • The first sample of pure hafnium was only a few milligrams and was a byproduct of an earlier attempt to isolate the element.
  • The discovery of hafnium filled the last remaining gap in the periodic table for a stable element.

Did You Know?

The element hafnium was the last element with a stable nucleus to be discovered. Its existence was theoretically predicted years before it was physically isolated, demonstrating the power of periodic law and atomic theory.
