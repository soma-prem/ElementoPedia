Discovery

Hassium was discovered in 1984 by a German research team led by **Peter Armbruster** and **Gottfried Münzenberg** at the GSI Helmholtz Centre for Heavy Ion Research in Darmstadt, Germany. They synthesized it by bombarding a lead-208 target with iron-58 ions. The name **Hassium** was approved in 1997, after a naming dispute, and it was named after the German state of **Hesse**, where the research institute is located.

Abundance & Sources

Hassium is a synthetic, radioactive element that does not exist in nature. It is produced in laboratories in minute quantities for scientific research.

Industrial Preparation

Hassium is produced by nuclear fusion reactions in a particle accelerator. A target of Lead-208 is bombarded with a beam of Iron-58 ions, causing the nuclei to fuse and create a new, heavier element. Only a few atoms are produced at a time.

Uses

  • Hassium has **no commercial applications** due to its extreme scarcity, high radioactivity, and very short half-life.
  • Its sole use is in **scientific research** to further understand the fundamental properties of the heaviest elements.

Interesting Facts

  • Hassium is a highly radioactive element with a very short half-life. The most stable isotope, Hassium-270, has a half-life of only about 21 seconds.
  • It is a very dense element, with an estimated density of about 40.7 g/cm³, which would make it one of the densest elements in the periodic table.
  • Hassium is the heaviest known member of the Group 8 elements, and its chemical properties are expected to be similar to its lighter homologues, osmium and ruthenium.

Did You Know?

The chemical properties of hassium have been studied on a single-atom basis. By creating volatile hassium tetroxide, scientists were able to determine that it behaves chemically like its lighter homologue, osmium, confirming the trends of the periodic table in the heaviest elements.
