Helium - Element Details

Symbol: He

Atomic Number: 2

Atomic Mass: 4.0026 u

Electron Configuration: 1s2

Group: 18 (Noble Gases)

Period: 1

Block: s-block

Category: Noble Gas

State at Room Temperature: Gas

Color: Colorless

Odor: Odorless

Melting Point: -272.2 °C

Boiling Point: -268.93 °C

Discovery

Helium was first discovered in 1868 by French astronomer Jules Janssen and English astronomer Joseph Norman Lockyer while observing a solar eclipse. It was first isolated on Earth in 1895 by Scottish chemist Sir William Ramsay.

Abundance & Sources

Helium is the second most abundant element in the universe, mostly found in stars and gas giants like Jupiter. On Earth, it is found in natural gas deposits, extracted through fractional distillation.

Industrial Preparation

Helium is primarily obtained through the cryogenic separation of natural gas containing helium-rich compounds. It cannot be synthesized economically and must be extracted from underground reserves.

Uses

  • Used in cryogenics, especially as liquid helium to cool superconducting magnets (e.g., in MRI scanners)
  • Used in balloons and airships as a non-flammable lifting gas
  • Used in welding as a shielding gas
  • Used in breathing mixtures for deep-sea diving (e.g., heliox)
  • Used in scientific research and particle accelerators

Interesting Facts

  • Helium is the second lightest and second most abundant element in the universe
  • It is completely inert and has no known chemical compounds under standard conditions
  • Helium is the only element that remains liquid near absolute zero without external pressure
  • It is used to cool the Large Hadron Collider at CERN
  • Inhaling helium temporarily changes the pitch of your voice (not safe in large quantities!)

Did You Know?

Helium is a non-renewable resource on Earth. Once released into the atmosphere, it escapes into space. Due to increasing demand and limited supply, helium conservation is becoming an important topic.
