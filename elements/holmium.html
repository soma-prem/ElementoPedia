Holmium - Element Details

Symbol: Ho

Atomic Number: 67

Atomic Mass: 164.93033 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f¹¹ 6s²

Group: 3 (Lanthanides)

Period: 6

Block: f-block

Category: Lanthanide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, metallic luster

Density: ~8.795 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1474 °C

Boiling Point: ~2695 °C

Discovery

Holmium was discovered in 1878 by Swiss chemists **Marc Delafontaine** and **Jacques-Louis Soret**. Independently, it was also discovered in 1879 by Swedish chemist **Per Teodor Cleve**. Cleve was working with a sample of erbium oxide from the mineral gadolinite when he isolated a new element which he named Holmium, after **Holmia**, the Latin name for Stockholm, Sweden.

Abundance & Sources

Holmium is a rare-earth element that is not found in its pure form in nature. It is typically found in rare-earth minerals such as monazite and xenotime, alongside other lanthanides. The primary sources for these minerals are located in China, the United States, and Australia.

Industrial Preparation

The industrial production of holmium involves a series of complex separation steps to isolate it from the other rare-earth elements in the ore. The separation is primarily achieved using solvent extraction or ion-exchange chromatography. The pure metal is then produced by the reduction of anhydrous holmium fluoride ($HoF_3$) with calcium metal at high temperatures.

Uses

  • Holmium has the highest magnetic moment of any naturally occurring element, making it useful in **superconducting magnets** and as a component in certain alloys.
  • **Holmium-doped YAG (Ho:YAG) lasers** are used in medical and dental applications, such as for the ablation of soft tissue in surgery and for breaking up kidney stones.
  • It is also a colorant for cubic zirconia and glass, giving them a yellow or reddish color.
  • Holmium is used in **nuclear reactor control rods** as a burnable poison to help control the nuclear fission process.

Interesting Facts

  • Holmium is a relatively soft, silvery-white metal that is malleable and ductile.
  • The name of the element is a tribute to Stockholm, Sweden, where the first isolation was made public by Cleve.
  • Holmium-doped YAG lasers are known for their specific infrared wavelength, which is strongly absorbed by water, allowing for precise cutting and cauterizing of tissue with minimal heat damage.

Did You Know?

The most common use of holmium in modern medicine is in high-tech surgical lasers. The specific wavelength of light produced by these lasers is ideal for a variety of surgical procedures, from arthroscopic surgery to the removal of tumors.
