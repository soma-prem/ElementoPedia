Hydrogen - Element Details

Symbol: H

Atomic Number: 1

Atomic Mass: 1.008 u

Electron Configuration: 1s1

Group: 1 (alkali metals, though technically not one)

Period: 1

Block: s-block

Category: Non-metal, Diatomic Gas

State at Room Temperature: Gas

Color: Colorless

Odor: Odorless

Melting Point: -259.16 °C

Boiling Point: -252.87 °C

Discovery

Hydrogen was first recognized as a distinct element by Henry Cavendish in 1766. He described it as "flammable air" which formed water upon combustion. Later, in 1783, Antoine Lavoisier named it *Hydrogen*, meaning "water-former".

Abundance & Sources

Hydrogen is the most abundant chemical substance in the universe, making up around 75% of all baryonic mass. It's found mainly in stars, including our Sun, where it undergoes nuclear fusion to produce energy. On Earth, it is found in water (H₂O), hydrocarbons, and most organic matter.

Industrial Preparation

Hydrogen is mainly produced by steam methane reforming (SMR), electrolysis of water, and from biomass.

Uses

  • Production of ammonia (NH₃) using the Haber process
  • Used in fuel cells for clean electricity generation
  • Used as a rocket fuel (liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen)
  • Used in the hydrogenation of oils and fats
  • Used in metallurgy as a reducing agent
  • Potential energy source in hydrogen-powered vehicles

Interesting Facts

  • Hydrogen is the lightest and most abundant element in the universe
  • It exists naturally as a diatomic molecule (H₂)
  • Hydrogen isotopes: Protium (¹H), Deuterium (²H), and Tritium (³H)
  • Stars fuse hydrogen into helium in a process that powers them
  • Hydrogen has metallic properties under extreme pressure

Did You Know?

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names: Protium (¹H - no neutron), Deuterium (²H - one neutron), and Tritium (³H - two neutrons, radioactive). Deuterium is used in nuclear reactors and research!
