Indium - Element Details

Symbol: In

Atomic Number: 49

Atomic Mass: 114.818 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d¹⁰ 5s² 5p¹

Group: 13 (Post-transition Metals)

Period: 5

Block: p-block

Category: Poor Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, lustrous metal

Density: ~7.31 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~156.6 °C

Boiling Point: ~2072 °C

Discovery

Indium was discovered in 1863 by German chemists Ferdinand Reich and Hieronymous Theodor Richter. They were using a spectroscope to look for thallium in zinc ores and instead found a brilliant indigo-blue spectral line, which led them to name the new element Indium after the Latin word "indicum," meaning indigo.

Abundance & Sources

Indium is a very soft, rare, and post-transition metal. It is not found in its native form but is a minor component of zinc ores, and sometimes lead, tin, and copper ores. Almost all of the world's indium is produced as a byproduct of zinc refining.

Industrial Preparation

Indium is primarily extracted as a byproduct of zinc smelting. The flue dust and residues from zinc smelting are treated chemically to separate the indium from the other metals. The final purification is achieved through electrolysis.

Uses

  • The largest use of indium is in the production of indium tin oxide (ITO), which is a transparent conductive coating used in LCD displays and touchscreen devices.
  • It is used in low-melting-point alloys, such as those used in fire sprinkler systems.
  • Indium is a component in some solders used in electronics, particularly for joining non-metallic materials.
  • In nuclear reactors, indium is used in control rods due to its ability to absorb neutrons.
  • It is also used to make mirrors that are highly resistant to corrosion and have a high reflectivity.

Interesting Facts

  • Indium is so soft that it can be scratched with a fingernail.
  • When a bar of indium is bent, it emits a high-pitched "tin cry" similar to the sound produced by tin.
  • The liquid range of indium is quite large, from its melting point of 156.6 °C to its boiling point of 2072 °C, making it useful in certain high-temperature applications.

Did You Know?

Indium tin oxide (ITO), a compound of indium, is essential for the function of modern smartphones and tablets. The transparent conductive layer on the screen that registers your touch is made of ITO.
