Iodine - Element Details

Symbol: I

Atomic Number: 53

Atomic Mass: 126.904 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d¹⁰ 5s² 5p⁵

Group: 17 (Halogens)

Period: 5

Block: p-block

Category: Nonmetal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Lustrous metallic gray-black solid; violet vapor

Density: ~4.933 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~113.7 °C

Boiling Point: ~184.3 °C

Discovery

Iodine was discovered in 1811 by French chemist Bernard Courtois. He was producing saltpeter for gunpowder manufacturing from seaweed ash. He noticed that when he added sulfuric acid to the ash, a beautiful violet vapor was produced. In 1813, the French chemist Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac identified this as a new element and named it "iode" after the Greek word "ioeides," meaning "violet-colored."

Abundance & Sources

Iodine is a relatively rare element in the Earth's crust. It is primarily found in seawater, where it is concentrated by sea life, especially kelp and other seaweeds. It is also found in brines from certain mineral springs and as a minor component in some saltpeter deposits in Chile.

Industrial Preparation

Iodine is commercially produced from several sources. The main method is from iodine-rich brines found in places like Japan and the United States. The brine is treated to oxidize the iodide ions to iodine, which is then purified. It can also be extracted from Chilean nitrate deposits.

Uses

  • Iodine is an essential element in human nutrition, necessary for the production of thyroid hormones. Iodized salt is widely used to prevent iodine deficiency disorders like goiter.
  • In the form of tincture of iodine and povidone-iodine, it is a powerful antiseptic used to clean wounds.
  • Radioactive iodine-131 is used in medicine to treat thyroid cancer and hyperthyroidism.
  • Silver iodide is used in photography and cloud seeding to produce rain.
  • Iodine is also used in catalysts, dyes, and in the production of some specialty chemicals.

Interesting Facts

  • Iodine is a halogen and is the heaviest stable halogen.
  • It is one of the few elements that sublimes easily, meaning it transitions directly from a solid to a gas without passing through a liquid phase.
  • The atomic number of Iodine (53) is less than the atomic number of Tellurium (52), which is an exception to the general trend in the periodic table.

Did You Know?

The characteristic deep purple or violet color of iodine vapor is a defining feature of the element and is what gave it its name. You can see this effect by gently heating iodine crystals in a flask.
