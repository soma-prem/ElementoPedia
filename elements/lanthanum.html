Lanthanum - Element Details

Symbol: La

Atomic Number: 57

Atomic Mass: 138.905 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 5d¹ 6s²

Group: 3 (Lanthanides)

Period: 6

Block: d-block

Category: Lanthanide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, malleable metal

Density: ~6.16 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~920 °C

Boiling Point: ~3464 °C

Discovery

Lanthanum was discovered in 1839 by Swedish chemist Carl Gustav Mosander. He was analyzing a sample of cerite, a mineral from which the element cerium had been isolated. He was able to separate a new substance he called "lanthana," which was a mixture of rare earth oxides. He named the new element "lanthanum" from the Greek word *lanthanein*, meaning "to lie hidden," because it was difficult to separate from the other rare earth elements.

Abundance & Sources

Lanthanum is the second most abundant of the rare-earth elements. It is not found in its pure form in nature but is a component of several rare-earth minerals, primarily bastnäsite and monazite. Major sources of these minerals include China, the United States, and Australia.

Industrial Preparation

The extraction and purification of lanthanum from its ores is a complex process. It is separated from other rare earth elements through techniques such as solvent extraction and ion-exchange chromatography. The pure metal is then produced by the reduction of anhydrous lanthanum fluoride ($LaF_3$) with calcium metal at high temperatures.

Uses

  • Lanthanum is used in alloys to make mischmetal, a key component in **flint for lighters**.
  • In camera and telescope lenses, lanthanum oxide is used to create high-refractive-index glass, which improves image clarity and reduces aberrations.
  • It is a key component in nickel-metal hydride (NiMH) batteries, widely used in hybrid vehicles.
  • Lanthanum oxide is used as a catalyst in petroleum cracking and as a component in ceramics and glass.
  • It is also used in some carbon arc lamps for studio lighting and projectors because it produces a bright, white light.

Interesting Facts

  • Lanthanum is the element that gives the lanthanide series its name and serves as its prototype.
  • It is a soft, malleable, silvery-white metal that is highly reactive and tarnishes rapidly when exposed to air.
  • When heated in air, powdered lanthanum can ignite and burn vigorously.

Did You Know?

The use of lanthanum in hybrid car batteries is so significant that it takes about 10 to 15 pounds of lanthanum to make the battery for a single Toyota Prius, making it a critical element in the green technology sector.
