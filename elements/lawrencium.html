Lawrencium - Element Details

Symbol: Lr

Atomic Number: 103

Atomic Mass: [262] u

Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f¹⁴ 7s² 7p¹

Group: 3 (Actinides)

Period: 7

Block: f-block

Category: Actinide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Unknown, likely silvery-white

Density: ~16.6 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1627 °C

Boiling Point: ~- °C

Discovery

Lawrencium was discovered in 1961 by a team of scientists led by **Albert Ghiorso** at the University of California, Berkeley. They synthesized it by bombarding a californium target with boron ions in a linear accelerator. The element was named in honor of **Ernest O. Lawrence**, the inventor of the cyclotron, a device that was instrumental in the discovery of many synthetic elements.

Abundance & Sources

Lawrencium is a synthetic, radioactive element that does not exist in nature. It is produced in very small quantities in laboratories and has no known natural abundance.

Industrial Preparation

Lawrencium is produced by nuclear fusion reactions in particle accelerators. A heavy ion beam, such as boron, is accelerated to high speeds and directed at a target of a lighter element, such as californium. The resulting product is a new, heavier element.

Uses

  • Lawrencium has **no commercial applications** due to its extreme scarcity, high radioactivity, and very short half-life.
  • Its sole use is in **scientific research** to understand the fundamental properties of the heaviest elements and to test theories of nuclear and atomic structure.

Interesting Facts

  • Lawrencium is the **last element of the actinide series**. Its discovery completed the filling of the f-block in the seventh period.
  • The most stable isotope, Lawrencium-262, has a half-life of only about 3.6 hours.
  • It was the first element to be named after a person who was still living at the time of its naming.

Did You Know?

The discovery of lawrencium was a key milestone in the synthesis of new elements. Its short half-life and the tiny amounts produced make it one of the most difficult elements to study, and its properties are still a subject of ongoing research.
