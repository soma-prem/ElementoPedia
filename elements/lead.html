Lead - Element Details

Symbol: Pb

Atomic Number: 82

Atomic Mass: 207.2 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s² 6p²

Group: 14 (Post-transition Metals)

Period: 6

Block: p-block

Category: Poor Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Bluish-white, tarnishes to dull gray

Density: ~11.34 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~327.5 °C

Boiling Point: ~1749 °C

Discovery

Lead has been known since ancient times and is one of the **"seven metals of antiquity"**. Its discovery is not attributed to a single person. Evidence of its use dates back to at least 7000 BCE in Turkey. The Romans used lead extensively for water pipes and other plumbing applications, which is the origin of its chemical symbol **Pb**, from the Latin word *plumbum*. This is also where the English word "plumber" comes from.

Abundance & Sources

Lead is a relatively common element in the Earth's crust. It is primarily found in the mineral **galena** (lead sulfide, $PbS$). It is also found in other minerals like cerussite and anglesite. The world's largest producers of lead are China, Australia, and the United States.

Industrial Preparation

The extraction of lead from galena is a straightforward process. The ore is first roasted in air to convert the lead sulfide into lead oxide ($PbO$). The oxide is then reduced with carbon in a blast furnace at high temperatures to produce molten lead metal. This crude lead is then refined to remove impurities.

Uses

  • The largest use of lead is in **lead-acid batteries** for vehicles, which accounts for most of its global consumption.
  • Due to its high density, it is an excellent material for **radiation shielding** from X-rays and gamma rays, making it vital for medical and nuclear applications.
  • Lead is also used in the production of ammunition (bullets) and in various alloys, such as **solder** and pewter.
  • Historically, it was used in paint pigments and gasoline additives, but these uses have been phased out in most countries due to its toxicity.

Interesting Facts

  • Lead is a heavy, soft, and malleable metal. It is highly toxic and exposure can cause neurological damage, especially in children.
  • It is a very dense element, but it is not as dense as some other heavy metals like gold, tungsten, or platinum.
  • Lead's atomic symbol, Pb, comes from the Latin word *plumbum*, which also gives us the word "plumbing."

Did You Know?

Despite being a known poison, lead remains an indispensable element in certain applications. Its high density and large atomic number are unique properties that make it highly effective at absorbing X-rays and gamma rays, a critical function in protecting people from radiation in medical and industrial settings.
