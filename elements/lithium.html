Lithium - Element Details

Symbol: Li

Atomic Number: 3

Atomic Mass: 6.94 u

Electron Configuration: 1s2 2s1

Group: 1 (Alkali Metals)

Period: 2

Block: s-block

Category: Alkali Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white

Odor: Odorless

Melting Point: 180.54 °C

Boiling Point: 1342 °C

Discovery

Lithium was discovered in 1817 by Swedish chemist Johan August Arfvedson while analyzing the mineral petalite. It was isolated in metallic form by William Thomas Brande and Humphry Davy shortly afterward using electrolysis.

Abundance & Sources

Lithium is not found freely in nature due to its high reactivity. It is found in trace amounts in nearly all igneous rocks and in mineral springs. Major commercial sources include minerals like spodumene and lepidolite, and lithium-rich brine lakes.

Industrial Preparation

Lithium is extracted from spodumene ores or from brine pools via evaporation and chemical treatment. Metallic lithium is produced by the electrolysis of a lithium chloride and potassium chloride mixture.

Uses

  • Used in rechargeable lithium-ion batteries for electronics and electric vehicles
  • Used as a mood stabilizer in psychiatric medicine (lithium salts)
  • Used in aerospace alloys due to its light weight
  • Used as a coolant in nuclear reactors
  • Used in the production of ceramics and glass

Interesting Facts

  • Lithium is the lightest metal and the least dense solid element
  • It reacts violently with water, producing hydrogen gas and heat
  • Its name comes from the Greek word "lithos", meaning "stone"
  • Lithium is essential for modern battery technology and renewable energy storage
  • It was one of the three elements produced in the Big Bang

Did You Know?

Lithium-ion batteries power smartphones, laptops, and electric cars. The demand for lithium has surged in recent years, leading to a global race for lithium mining and sustainable recycling technologies.
