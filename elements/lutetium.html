Lutetium - Element Details

Symbol: Lu

Atomic Number: 71

Atomic Mass: 174.9668 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹ 6s²

Group: 3 (Lanthanides)

Period: 6

Block: d-block

Category: Lanthanide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, corrosion-resistant metal

Density: ~9.841 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1663 °C

Boiling Point: ~3402 °C

Discovery

Lutetium was discovered independently in 1907 by three scientists: French chemist **Georges Urbain**, Austrian mineralogist **Carl Auer von Welsbach**, and American chemist **Charles James**. Urbain named it after **Lutetia**, the ancient Roman name for Paris. It was the last of the naturally occurring rare-earth elements to be discovered.

Abundance & Sources

Lutetium is the rarest and most expensive of the rare-earth elements. It is never found in its pure form in nature. It is found in trace amounts in rare-earth minerals such as xenotime and monazite, often alongside the element yttrium.

Industrial Preparation

Due to its rarity and high cost, lutetium is produced in very small quantities. The industrial process involves a series of complex solvent extraction steps to isolate it from the other rare-earth elements. The pure metal is then produced by the reduction of anhydrous lutetium fluoride ($LuF_3$) with calcium metal at high temperatures.

Uses

  • Lutetium is used as a **catalyst in petroleum cracking** and in some hydrogenation processes.
  • Due to its stable neutron absorption, the isotope Lutetium-176 is used as a detector for **neutrino research** and in certain types of radiation detectors.
  • The radioisotope **Lutetium-177** is used in medical therapies for certain types of cancer, a field known as targeted radionuclide therapy.
  • It is also used as a component in certain **high-refractive-index glasses** and in ceramic garnets for specialized electronics.

Interesting Facts

  • Lutetium is the last element in the lanthanide series and is considered a d-block element due to its electron configuration.
  • It is the densest, hardest, and has one of the highest melting points of all the lanthanides.
  • Because of its very small natural abundance and difficulty in separation, lutetium is one of the most expensive elements in the world.

Did You Know?

Lutetium's electron configuration, with a completely filled 4f subshell and a single electron in the 5d subshell, is a key reason why it is considered the end of the lanthanide series and the transition to the next row of d-block elements.
