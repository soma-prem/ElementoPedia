Molybdenum - Element Details

Symbol: Mo

Atomic Number: 42

Atomic Mass: 95.95 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d⁵ 5s¹

Group: 6 (Transition Metals)

Period: 5

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, lustrous metal

Density: ~10.2 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~2623 °C

Boiling Point: ~4639 °C

Discovery

Molybdenum was discovered in 1778 by Swedish chemist Carl Wilhelm Scheele in a mineral known as molybdenite. However, the element was first isolated by Peter Jacob Hjelm in 1781. The name is derived from the Greek word "molybdos," meaning "lead," as molybdenite was often confused with lead ore.

Abundance & Sources

Molybdenum is a relatively rare element. Its primary ore is molybdenite ($MoS_2$). It is also a significant byproduct of copper mining. The major producers of molybdenum are China, Chile, and the United States.

Industrial Preparation

Molybdenum is extracted from molybdenite by roasting the ore in air to produce molybdenum trioxide ($MoO_3$). The trioxide is then reduced to molybdenum powder using hydrogen gas. The powder can be further processed into metal billets through sintering or by melting in a vacuum.

Uses

  • Used as a major alloying agent in steel and cast iron to increase strength, hardness, and corrosion resistance at high temperatures.
  • Molybdenum disulfide ($MoS_2$) is a key component in dry lubricants due to its low friction properties.
  • Used in high-temperature heating elements, furnace parts, and electrodes.
  • Molybdenum is an essential trace element in biology, playing a role in the function of various enzymes.

Interesting Facts

  • Molybdenum has one of the highest melting points of all pure elements, second only to tungsten and tantalum.
  • It is an essential element for plant nutrition and is often a component of fertilizers.
  • In its pure form, molybdenum is soft and ductile, but it becomes harder and more resistant to heat when alloyed with other metals.

Did You Know?

The iron and steel industries account for over 75% of global molybdenum consumption. Its ability to enhance the strength and durability of steel at extreme temperatures makes it indispensable for components in industrial machinery, aircraft, and armor.
