Neodymium - Element Details

Symbol: Nd

Atomic Number: 60

Atomic Mass: 144.242 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f⁴ 6s²

Group: 3 (Lanthanides)

Period: 6

Block: f-block

Category: Lanthanide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white metal with a yellowish tinge

Density: ~7.01 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1021 °C

Boiling Point: ~3074 °C

Discovery

Neodymium was discovered in 1885 by Austrian chemist **Carl Auer von Welsbach**. He successfully separated the element from a material called didymium, which was previously thought to be a single element. He also discovered praseodymium from the same material. He named it Neodymium, which comes from the Greek words *neos* (meaning "new") and *didymos* (meaning "twin"), reflecting its discovery as the "new twin" to praseodymium and its relationship to didymium.

Abundance & Sources

Neodymium is a moderately abundant rare-earth element, found in the Earth's crust in concentrations comparable to that of copper or nickel. It is never found in its pure form in nature. The primary commercial sources are the minerals bastnäsite and monazite, found mainly in China, Australia, and the United States.

Industrial Preparation

The industrial process for obtaining neodymium involves a series of complex steps to separate it from other rare-earth elements in the ore. The separation is primarily done using solvent extraction or ion-exchange chromatography. The pure metal is then produced by the electrolysis of molten neodymium fluoride ($NdF_3$) or neodymium chloride ($NdCl_3$) with calcium metal at high temperatures.

Uses

  • The most significant use of neodymium is in the production of **neodymium magnets**, which are the strongest and most widely used type of rare-earth magnets.
  • Neodymium magnets are essential in many modern technologies, including headphones, microphones, computer hard drives, and electric vehicle motors.
  • Neodymium compounds are used to color glass and ceramics in various shades from violet to red, and to create a special glass that changes color under different lighting.
  • Neodymium-doped yttrium aluminum garnet (Nd:YAG) crystals are a crucial component in high-power lasers, which are used in eye surgery, cosmetic surgery, and industrial applications.
  • It is also a component in didymium glass, used for protective goggles by welders and glassblowers.

Interesting Facts

  • Neodymium magnets can be more than 10 times stronger than conventional iron magnets of the same size.
  • The beautiful violet-red color of neodymium glass is a result of the element's unique light absorption properties.
  • Neodymium metal tarnishes readily in air, forming a flaky reddish-purple oxide coating that doesn't protect the metal from further oxidation.

Did You Know?

The high demand for neodymium magnets in consumer electronics, wind turbines, and electric vehicles has made neodymium a strategically important and valuable element, and its price can be quite volatile.
