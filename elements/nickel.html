Nickel - Element Details

Symbol: Ni

Atomic Number: 28

Atomic Mass: 58.6934 u

Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d⁸ 4s²

Group: 10 (Transition Metals)

Period: 4

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Lustrous, silvery-white metal with a slight golden tinge

Density: ~8.908 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1455 °C

Boiling Point: ~2913 °C

Discovery

Nickel was discovered by Swedish chemist Axel Fredrik Cronstedt in 1751. He initially mistook a mineral for a copper ore, but later extracted a new metal from it, which he named nickel (from the German "Kupfernickel", meaning "copper demon" or "goblin's copper", referring to its deceptive appearance).

Abundance & Sources

Nickel is the fifth most abundant element on Earth by mass, but is concentrated mostly in the Earth's core. In the crust, it is the 22nd most abundant element. It is found in minerals like pentlandite, nickeliferous limonite, and garnierite. Major producers include Indonesia, Philippines, and Canada.

Industrial Preparation

Nickel is extracted from its ores through various processes depending on the ore type, including pyrometallurgical methods (roasting and smelting) and hydrometallurgical methods (leaching and solvent extraction), often followed by electrolytic refining.

Uses

  • Primary component in stainless steel and other alloys (e.g., Inconel, Monel) for their corrosion resistance and high-temperature strength.
  • Used in coinage and as a plating material (nickel plating) for corrosion protection and decorative purposes.
  • Important in rechargeable batteries, particularly nickel-cadmium (Ni-Cd) and nickel-metal hydride (Ni-MH) batteries.
  • Used as a catalyst in various industrial chemical processes, such as hydrogenation.

Interesting Facts

  • Nickel is one of only three naturally occurring ferromagnetic elements, alongside iron and cobalt.
  • It has good ductility and malleability, making it easily workable.
  • Around 20% of the nickel produced annually goes into plating applications.

Did You Know?

Meteorites often contain a significant amount of nickel, sometimes as high as 5-20%, which is one of the ways to identify a meteorite from terrestrial rocks.
