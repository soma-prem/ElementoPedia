Niobium - Element Details

Symbol: Nb

Atomic Number: 41

Atomic Mass: 92.906 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d⁴ 5s¹

Group: 5 (Transition Metals)

Period: 5

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, lustrous metal

Density: ~8.57 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~2477 °C

Boiling Point: ~4744 °C

Discovery

Niobium was discovered in 1801 by English chemist Charles Hatchett, who named it columbium. In 1844, German chemist Heinrich Rose discovered what he believed was a new element and named it niobium after Niobe, the daughter of Tantalus in Greek mythology. It was later confirmed that columbium and niobium were the same element. The name niobium was officially adopted by IUPAC in 1949.

Abundance & Sources

Niobium is found almost exclusively in the mineral series columbite–tantalite, or "coltan," often alongside tantalum. The primary commercial source is the mineral pyrochlore. Brazil and Canada are major producers.

Industrial Preparation

Niobium is obtained from its ore, primarily pyrochlore. The ore is first processed to produce niobium pentoxide ($Nb_2O_5$), which is then reduced with aluminum or carbon to yield niobium powder. The powder is then purified by electron-beam melting or vacuum sintering.

Uses

  • Used in high-strength, low-alloy (HSLA) steels to increase strength and toughness, particularly in pipelines and structural components.
  • A major alloying element in nickel-based superalloys for jet engines and rocket assemblies due to its high-temperature strength.
  • Niobium-titanium and niobium-tin alloys are superconductors used to create powerful magnets in MRI machines and particle accelerators.
  • Used in jewelry and some surgical implants due to its hypoallergenic and non-toxic properties.

Interesting Facts

  • Niobium is a superconductor at low temperatures.
  • It is highly resistant to corrosion but will oxidize in air at temperatures above 400°C.
  • It can be anodized to produce a wide range of iridescent colors, making it popular for jewelry.

Did You Know?

Niobium's use in superalloys for aerospace applications was crucial for the development of early space programs like the Apollo Service Module and is still used in modern rocket engines, such as those made by SpaceX.
