Nobelium - Element Details

Symbol: No

Atomic Number: 102

Atomic Mass: [259] u

Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f¹⁴ 7s²

Group: 3 (Actinides)

Period: 7

Block: f-block

Category: Actinide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Unknown, likely silvery-white

Density: ~9.9 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~827 °C

Boiling Point: ~1400 °C

Discovery

Nobelium was discovered in 1958 by a team of American scientists led by **Glenn T. Seaborg**, **Albert Ghiorso**, and **Torbjørn Sikkeland** at the University of California, Berkeley. They synthesized it by bombarding a curium-246 target with carbon-12 ions. The element was named in honor of **Alfred Nobel**, the Swedish chemist who invented dynamite and established the Nobel Prizes.

Abundance & Sources

Nobelium is a synthetic, radioactive element that does not exist in nature. It is produced in very small quantities in nuclear reactors and laboratories for scientific research.

Industrial Preparation

Nobelium is produced in a particle accelerator by bombarding a curium-246 target with carbon-12 ions. The discovery was a complex process of identifying a few atoms of the new element from the reaction products.

Uses

  • Nobelium has **no commercial applications** due to its extreme scarcity, high radioactivity, and short half-life.
  • Its sole use is in **scientific research**, particularly to understand the properties of the actinide series and to produce heavier, transuranic elements.

Interesting Facts

  • Nobelium was the tenth transuranic element to be discovered.
  • The most stable isotope, Nobelium-259, has a half-life of 58 minutes.
  • There was a prior, incorrect claim of discovery by a Swedish team in 1957, which was later retracted. The American team's work was the one officially recognized.

Did You Know?

Nobelium is one of the more difficult synthetic elements to study. Its short half-life means that any experiments must be conducted very quickly after its production, often on a single atom at a time.
