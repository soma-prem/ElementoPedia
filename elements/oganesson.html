Oganesson - Element Details

Symbol: Og

Atomic Number: 118

Atomic Mass: [294] u

Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f¹⁴ 6d¹⁰ 7s² 7p⁶

Group: 18 (Noble Gases)

Period: 7

Block: p-block

Category: Noble Gas (predicted)

State at Room Temperature: Gas (predicted)

Color: Unknown

Density: ~4.9 g/L (at STP)

Melting Point: ~52 °C

Boiling Point: ~80 °C

Discovery

Oganesson was discovered in 2002 by a Russian-American team at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research (JINR) in Dubna, Russia. The team was led by **Yuri Oganessian** and included scientists from the Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory. The element was named in honor of **Yuri Oganessian**, a leading scientist in the field of superheavy element research.

Abundance & Sources

Oganesson is a synthetic, radioactive element that does not exist in nature. It is produced in laboratories in minute quantities for scientific research. Only a few atoms have ever been synthesized.

Industrial Preparation

Oganesson is produced by nuclear fusion reactions in a particle accelerator. A target of Californium-249 is bombarded with a beam of Calcium-48 ions, causing the nuclei to fuse and create an atom of oganesson. The discovery was confirmed by identifying the decay chain of the new element.

Uses

  • Oganesson has **no commercial applications** due to its extreme scarcity, high radioactivity, and very short half-life.
  • Its sole use is in **scientific research** to further understand the fundamental properties of the heaviest elements and the limits of the periodic table.

Interesting Facts

  • Oganesson is a highly radioactive element with a very short half-life. The most stable isotope, Oganesson-294, has a half-life of only about 0.89 milliseconds.
  • It is the **last element in the periodic table**.
  • Although it is in the noble gas group, theoretical predictions suggest that due to **relativistic effects**, oganesson may be a semi-metal or even a solid at room temperature, which is a major departure from the properties of its lighter homologues.

Did You Know?

The discovery of oganesson completed the seventh period of the periodic table, providing valuable data for a new generation of scientists to explore the chemistry of the superheavy elements and the limits of nuclear stability.
