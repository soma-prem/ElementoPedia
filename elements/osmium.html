Osmium - Element Details

Symbol: Os

Atomic Number: 76

Atomic Mass: 190.23 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d⁶ 6s²

Group: 8 (Transition Metals)

Period: 6

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal, Platinum Group Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Bluish-white, hard, and brittle metal

Density: ~22.59 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~3033 °C

Boiling Point: ~5012 °C

Discovery

Osmium was discovered in 1803 by English chemist **Smithson Tennant**. He found it in the black, insoluble residue left over after dissolving platinum ore in a mixture of nitric and hydrochloric acid (aqua regia). He named it "osmium" from the Greek word *osme*, meaning "**smell**," because of the distinctive, pungent, and volatile odor of its oxide, osmium tetroxide ($OsO_4$).

Abundance & Sources

Osmium is one of the rarest elements in the Earth's crust. It is a member of the platinum group metals and is almost always found in its natural state as an alloy with other platinum group elements, particularly in deposits containing iridium. It is obtained commercially as a byproduct of nickel and copper refining.

Industrial Preparation

Osmium is recovered from the anode slimes of nickel and copper refining. The osmium is separated from the other platinum group metals by converting it to the highly volatile and toxic **osmium tetroxide** ($OsO_4$) through an oxidative process. The gas is then absorbed into a caustic solution and reduced to the pure osmium metal powder.

Uses

  • Osmium is used in very hard alloys with other platinum group metals for applications requiring extreme durability, such as **fountain pen tips**, electrical contacts, and instrument pivots.
  • Because of its toxicity, **osmium tetroxide** is used as a staining agent for biological samples in microscopy, as it is effective at fixing and contrasting lipids.
  • Its extreme hardness and high melting point make it useful in certain specialized high-temperature and high-pressure applications.
  • Osmium has also been used in some high-performance magnets and electrical components.

Interesting Facts

  • Osmium is the **densest stable element** known, with a density of 22.59 g/cm³, just slightly higher than its neighbor, iridium.
  • It has a unique bluish-white appearance and is extremely hard and brittle, making it difficult to fabricate.
  • The volatile oxide of osmium, osmium tetroxide, is highly toxic and can cause serious damage to eyes and lungs. This makes the handling of osmium a hazardous process.

Did You Know?

Due to its extreme hardness and resistance to wear, osmium was historically used in the tips of high-quality fountain pen nibs, especially the famous "Parker 51," to provide a long-lasting, smooth writing experience.
