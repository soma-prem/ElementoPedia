Palladium - Element Details

Symbol: Pd

Atomic Number: 46

Atomic Mass: 106.42 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d¹⁰

Group: 10 (Transition Metals)

Period: 5

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal, Platinum Group Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white metal

Density: ~12.02 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1554.9 °C

Boiling Point: ~2963 °C

Discovery

Palladium was discovered in 1803 by English chemist William Hyde Wollaston. He found the element while working with crude platinum ore from South America. He named it after the asteroid Pallas, which had been discovered two years earlier.

Abundance & Sources

Palladium is a rare element, found in nature as a free metal and in alloys with other platinum group metals in deposits across the globe. The largest deposits are in Russia, South Africa, Canada, and the United States. It is also found in some nickel and copper ores.

Industrial Preparation

Palladium is mainly produced as a byproduct of the mining and refining of other metals, particularly nickel and copper. It is separated from the other platinum group metals through a complex process involving dissolving the ore in aqua regia (a highly corrosive acid), followed by a series of chemical precipitation steps to isolate the pure palladium.

Uses

  • Approximately 85% of palladium is used in catalytic converters for vehicles to reduce harmful exhaust emissions.
  • It is used in dentistry for dental fillings, crowns, and bridges due to its resistance to corrosion.
  • Palladium is used in jewelry as a part of white gold alloys and in fine watchmaking.
  • It is an important catalyst in various chemical reactions, including those used in the production of synthetic fibers and fertilizers.
  • Due to its ability to absorb large amounts of hydrogen, it is used in hydrogen purification and storage technologies.

Interesting Facts

  • Palladium is a platinum group metal that is notable for its unique electron configuration, being the only element to have a completely filled d-subshell and an empty s-subshell in its ground state.
  • It has the lowest melting point of all the platinum group metals.
  • Palladium can absorb up to 900 times its own volume of hydrogen, a property that makes it a key component in hydrogen fuel cell technology.

Did You Know?

The price of palladium can be quite volatile and, at times, has exceeded the price of gold, particularly driven by demand from the automotive industry for catalytic converters.
