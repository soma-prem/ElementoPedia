Plutonium - Element Details

Symbol: Pu

Atomic Number: 94

Atomic Mass: [244] u

Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f⁶ 7s²

Group: 3 (Actinides)

Period: 7

Block: f-block

Category: Actinide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, tarnishes to a dull gray or yellowish color

Density: ~19.82 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~640 °C

Boiling Point: ~3228 °C

Discovery

Plutonium was discovered in 1940 by a team of American scientists led by **Glenn T. Seaborg**, including **Edwin McMillan**, **Joseph W. Kennedy**, and **Arthur C. Wahl**. They synthesized it by bombarding uranium with deuterons (nuclei of deuterium, an isotope of hydrogen) at the University of California, Berkeley. They named it after the dwarf planet **Pluto**, following the trend of naming new transuranic elements after the planets Uranus (for uranium) and Neptune (for neptunium).

Abundance & Sources

Plutonium is an extremely rare, radioactive element. It is found in trace amounts in uranium ores as a product of natural radioactive decay. For all practical purposes, plutonium is a synthetic element that is produced in nuclear reactors.

Industrial Preparation

The industrial production of plutonium is a byproduct of the nuclear fission of uranium fuel. In a nuclear reactor, uranium-238 absorbs neutrons to become uranium-239, which then decays to neptunium and finally to **plutonium-239**. Plutonium is then chemically separated and recovered from the spent nuclear fuel in a process known as reprocessing.

Uses

  • The primary use of plutonium is as **nuclear fuel** in certain types of nuclear reactors.
  • The isotope **Plutonium-239** is the fissile component in many modern **nuclear weapons**.
  • The isotope **Plutonium-238** is used in **radioisotope thermoelectric generators (RTGs)**, which are compact, long-lasting power sources for spacecraft and other remote equipment.

Interesting Facts

  • Plutonium is a highly radioactive and toxic heavy metal. Due to its radioactivity, it is warm to the touch.
  • It is an extremely dense element, only slightly less dense than gold, and can exist in six different solid phases (allotropes) with varying crystal structures and densities.
  • A common isotope, Plutonium-239, has a half-life of 24,110 years, making it dangerous for centuries to come.

Did You Know?

Plutonium-238 has a half-life of 87.7 years, which means it continuously generates a significant amount of heat from its decay. This property makes it the ideal fuel for RTGs, providing a reliable power source for deep-space missions like Voyager, Galileo, and Cassini, which traveled too far from the Sun to use solar power.
