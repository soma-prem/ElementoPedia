Praseodymium - Element Details

Symbol: Pr

Atomic Number: 59

Atomic Mass: 140.90766 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f³ 6s²

Group: 3 (Lanthanides)

Period: 6

Block: f-block

Category: Lanthanide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, malleable, and ductile metal

Density: ~6.77 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~935 °C

Boiling Point: ~3520 °C

Discovery

Praseodymium was discovered in 1885 by Austrian chemist Carl Auer von Welsbach. He separated a substance known as didymium into two new elements: praseodymium and neodymium. He named praseodymium from the Greek words *prasios* (meaning "green") and *didymos* (meaning "twin"), due to the distinct green color of its salts and its close relationship to neodymium.

Abundance & Sources

Praseodymium is one of the more abundant rare-earth elements, although it is never found in its pure form in nature. It is a component of several rare-earth minerals, primarily bastnäsite and monazite. Major sources of these minerals include China, the United States, and Australia.

Industrial Preparation

The separation of praseodymium from other rare-earth elements is a complex process. It is typically achieved through solvent extraction or ion-exchange chromatography. The pure metal is then produced by heating praseodymium fluoride ($PrF_3$) with calcium metal at high temperatures.

Uses

  • Praseodymium is used to color glass and ceramics a distinct yellow-green.
  • It is a crucial component in **didymium glass**, which is used in welders' goggles because it absorbs the intense yellow light produced by sodium.
  • In alloys with other rare-earth elements, it is used to make powerful **neodymium-iron-boron magnets**.
  • It is used in carbon arc lights to produce a very bright, high-quality light for the film industry and projectors.
  • Praseodymium oxide is a component in some types of pigments and catalysts.

Interesting Facts

  • Praseodymium is a soft, malleable, and ductile metal that is resistant to corrosion in air, which is unusual for a rare-earth metal.
  • The beautiful green color of praseodymium compounds is a result of the light absorption by the f-orbital electrons.
  • The term "didymium," from which praseodymium was separated, is still used in some contexts to refer to a mixture of praseodymium and neodymium.

Did You Know?

Didymium glass, which contains praseodymium, is not just for welders. It is also used by glassblowers and jewelers to protect their eyes from the bright yellow light of molten glass, allowing them to see their work more clearly.
