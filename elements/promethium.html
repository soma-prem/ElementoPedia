Promethium - Element Details

Symbol: Pm

Atomic Number: 61

Atomic Mass: [145] u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f⁵ 6s²

Group: 3 (Lanthanides)

Period: 6

Block: f-block

Category: Lanthanide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Metallic, sometimes with a greenish or bluish glow

Density: ~7.26 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1042 °C

Boiling Point: ~3000 °C

Discovery

Promethium was discovered in 1945 by American chemists **Jacob A. Marinsky**, **Lawrence E. Glendenin**, and **Charles D. Coryell** at Oak Ridge National Laboratory in Tennessee. They isolated and chemically identified the element from the fission products of uranium fuel. They named the element after the Greek Titan **Prometheus**, who stole fire from the gods, symbolizing humanity's use of the power of nuclear fission.

Abundance & Sources

Promethium is one of the rarest elements. It does not exist in significant quantities in the Earth's crust due to all of its isotopes being radioactive with relatively short half-lives. It is a fission product in nuclear reactors, and this is the only practical source for its production.

Industrial Preparation

The industrial preparation of promethium is a purely synthetic process. The element is produced by separating it from the fission products of uranium fuel rods that have been used in nuclear reactors. The most abundant and useful isotope, Promethium-147, is extracted from the waste using ion-exchange resins and other chemical separation techniques.

Uses

  • Promethium-147 is used in **atomic batteries** for devices that need a long-lasting, low-power source, such as pacemakers and some scientific instruments.
  • It is a beta radiation source used in thickness gauges to measure the thickness of materials.
  • Promethium compounds were once used in a type of **luminous paint** for watches and signal lights, but due to its radioactivity, it has largely been replaced by safer alternatives.
  • In the past, it was used as a portable X-ray source for certain applications.

Interesting Facts

  • Promethium is the only lanthanide and one of only two elements below bismuth (the other being technetium) that has no stable isotopes. All of its isotopes are radioactive.
  • Due to its radioactivity, it glows in the dark with a faint greenish-blue light.
  • The atomic mass of promethium is often given in brackets, [145], to indicate that it is a synthetic element with no stable isotopes and that the number refers to the mass of the most stable isotope.

Did You Know?

Promethium was one of the last elements on the periodic table to be discovered. Its existence was theoretically predicted years before its discovery, and the search for it was a major scientific challenge.
