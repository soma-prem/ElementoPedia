Radium - Element Details

Symbol: Ra

Atomic Number: 88

Atomic Mass: [226] u

Electron Configuration: [Rn] 7s²

Group: 2 (Alkaline Earth Metals)

Period: 7

Block: s-block

Category: Alkaline Earth Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, highly radioactive metal

Density: ~5.5 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~700 °C

Boiling Point: ~1737 °C

Discovery

Radium was discovered in 1898 by Polish-French scientist **Marie Curie** and her husband **Pierre Curie**. They isolated it from the uranium ore pitchblende after a long and difficult process of fractional crystallization. They named the new element "radium" from the Latin word *radius*, meaning "**ray**," in reference to its powerful radioactivity.

Abundance & Sources

Radium is an extremely rare element. It is a product of the radioactive decay of uranium, so it is found in minute amounts in all uranium ores. It is estimated that a ton of uranium ore contains only about one-seventh of a gram of radium.

Industrial Preparation

The industrial production of radium is no longer common due to its dangers and limited demand. Historically, it was extracted from uranium ore through a highly labor-intensive process of fractional crystallization. This method required processing several tons of ore to obtain a single gram of pure radium.

Uses

  • Historically, radium was used in **luminous paint** for watch dials, instrument panels, and clock faces, causing a continuous glow from its radioactivity. This practice was discontinued due to the severe health risks associated with radium exposure.
  • In medicine, radium was once used for **radiotherapy** to treat certain types of cancer.
  • Radium is also used as a source of neutrons for research and in medical radiation devices.

Interesting Facts

  • Radium is a highly radioactive alkaline earth metal. It is over a **million times more radioactive** than uranium.
  • When exposed to air, pure radium turns black due to the formation of its nitride.
  • The intense radiation from radium causes the surrounding air to glow faintly blue in the dark, a phenomenon that captivated the public after its discovery but was later understood to be a sign of its dangerous nature.

Did You Know?

For a time after its discovery, radium was celebrated as a miracle cure and was added to many consumer products, from toothpaste to bottled water. This widespread use led to many cases of severe health problems and death, a tragic lesson about the dangers of radioactivity.
