Radon - Element Details

Symbol: Rn

Atomic Number: 86

Atomic Mass: [222] u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s² 6p⁶

Group: 18 (Noble Gases)

Period: 6

Block: p-block

Category: Noble Gas

State at Room Temperature: Gas

Color: Colorless gas

Density: ~9.73 g/L (at STP)

Melting Point: -71 °C

Boiling Point: -61.7 °C

Discovery

Radon was discovered in 1900 by German chemist **Friedrich Ernst Dorn**. While studying the decay chain of radium, he observed that radium compounds emitted a new radioactive gas. He initially named it "radium emanation," and it was later isolated and confirmed as a new element. in 1923, the name was officially changed to "radon" to reflect its origin from the element radium.

Abundance & Sources

Radon is a naturally occurring radioactive noble gas. It is a product of the radioactive decay of radium, which in turn is a decay product of uranium and thorium. Radon is found in trace amounts in uranium-rich rocks and soil and can seep up from the ground into the atmosphere.

Industrial Preparation

Due to its short half-life and radioactivity, radon is not produced industrially in bulk quantities. It is typically collected by trapping the radioactive gas released from the decay of radium in sealed containers. This method is used to gather minute amounts for scientific or medical purposes.

Uses

  • Radon has very limited practical applications due to its high radioactivity. Historically, it was used in **radiotherapy** for treating some types of cancer.
  • It is primarily used today in **scientific research**, particularly as a tracer in hydrology to study the movement of groundwater and ocean currents.
  • Radon is also used in some laboratories as a source of alpha radiation.

Interesting Facts

  • Radon is a **colorless, odorless, and tasteless gas**, making it difficult to detect without specialized equipment.
  • It is the **heaviest of the noble gases** and is one of the densest known gases at standard temperature and pressure.
  • Radon is considered a major indoor air pollutant and is the **leading cause of lung cancer** in non-smokers. Its radioactive decay products can be inhaled and cause damage to lung tissue.

Did You Know?

Radon gas naturally seeps into homes and buildings from the ground through cracks in the foundation. In poorly ventilated areas, it can accumulate to dangerous levels. Testing for radon is a common practice in many countries to ensure indoor air quality and safety.
