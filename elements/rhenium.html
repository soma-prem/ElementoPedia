Rhenium - Element Details

Symbol: Re

Atomic Number: 75

Atomic Mass: 186.207 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d⁵ 6s²

Group: 7 (Transition Metals)

Period: 6

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, metallic luster

Density: ~21.02 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~3186 °C

Boiling Point: ~5597 °C

Discovery

Rhenium was discovered in 1925 by German chemists **Walter Noddack**, **Ida Tacke**, and **Otto Berg**. They identified the element in platinum ores and the mineral columbite using X-ray spectroscopy. They named it "rhenium" after **Rhenus**, the Latin name for the Rhine River in Germany, as a tribute to their homeland. It was the last naturally occurring element with a stable isotope to be discovered.

Abundance & Sources

Rhenium is one of the rarest elements in the Earth's crust, with an estimated average concentration of about 1 part per billion. It is never found in its native form. Commercially, it is obtained as a minor byproduct of the mining and processing of **molybdenum and copper** ores, as it is found in small amounts within these minerals.

Industrial Preparation

The industrial production of rhenium is primarily a byproduct of molybdenum smelting. During the roasting of molybdenum sulfide ores, rhenium is oxidized and becomes a volatile compound that is captured in the flue dust and gases. The rhenium is then recovered from this dust through a series of chemical processes, purified, and finally reduced with hydrogen to produce the pure metal.

Uses

  • Rhenium is a key component in **high-temperature superalloys** used for parts of jet engines, such as turbine blades, which must withstand extreme heat and stress.
  • A **platinum-rhenium alloy** is an extremely effective catalyst used in the production of high-octane, lead-free gasoline.
  • Due to its high melting point and resistance to wear, it is used in filaments for mass spectrometers, thermocouples for measuring high temperatures, and electrical contacts.
  • Rhenium is also used in X-ray machines and in some rocket engines.

Interesting Facts

  • Rhenium has the **third-highest melting point** of all elements (after tungsten and carbon) and the **highest boiling point** of any element.
  • It is one of the densest elements, exceeded only by platinum, iridium, and osmium.
  • Although it is an extremely hard and dense metal, annealed rhenium is very ductile and can be bent, coiled, or rolled.

Did You Know?

Rhenium's high resistance to thermal creep (deforming under high heat) makes it an indispensable component in superalloys for modern jet engines. A typical jet engine contains about 3% to 6% rhenium in its turbine blades, making it a strategically important and valuable element.
