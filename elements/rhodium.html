Rhodium - Element Details

Symbol: Rh

Atomic Number: 45

Atomic Mass: 102.906 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d⁸ 5s¹

Group: 9 (Transition Metals)

Period: 5

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal, Platinum Group Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, highly reflective metal

Density: ~12.41 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1964 °C

Boiling Point: ~3695 °C

Discovery

Rhodium was discovered in 1803 by English chemist William Hyde Wollaston in London. He isolated the element from a piece of platinum ore, specifically one that had been treated to remove platinum and palladium. He named it rhodium after the Greek word 'rhodon', meaning rose, due to the rose-red color of some of its compounds.

Abundance & Sources

Rhodium is one of the rarest and most expensive precious metals. It is primarily found in platinum group mineral deposits, often alloyed with other metals like palladium and platinum. Major sources include the Ural Mountains, North and South America, and particularly the platinum-rich mines in South Africa.

Industrial Preparation

The extraction of rhodium is a complex and labor-intensive process, as it is a byproduct of nickel and copper mining and refining. The separation from other platinum group metals involves a series of chemical reactions, precipitation steps, and ion-exchange chromatography to achieve high purity.

Uses

  • The primary use of rhodium is in catalytic converters for automobiles, where it helps reduce nitrogen oxide (NOx) emissions.
  • It is used as an alloying agent to harden platinum and palladium, making them suitable for high-wear applications.
  • Rhodium is electroplated onto jewelry and mirrors to provide a durable, highly reflective, and tarnish-resistant finish.
  • In the chemical industry, rhodium is used as a catalyst in processes such as the carbonylation of methanol to produce acetic acid.
  • Because of its low electrical resistance and stable contact resistance, it is used in some electrical contacts.

Interesting Facts

  • Rhodium is an exceptionally bright and reflective metal, making it an excellent choice for plating mirrors and jewelry.
  • It is highly resistant to corrosion and is not attacked by most acids.
  • Rhodium is considered the rarest non-radioactive precious metal, and its price can fluctuate dramatically due to market demand.

Did You Know?

Because of its high value, there have been a number of thefts of catalytic converters from cars, primarily to get the rhodium, palladium, and platinum they contain.
