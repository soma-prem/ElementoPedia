Rubidium - Element Details

Symbol: Rb

Atomic Number: 37

Atomic Mass: 85.4678 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 5s¹

Group: 1 (Alkali Metals)

Period: 5

Block: s-block

Category: Alkali Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid (but very soft and highly reactive)

Color: Silvery-white, lustrous

Density: ~1.532 g/cm³

Melting Point: 39.3 °C

Boiling Point: 688 °C

Discovery

Rubidium was discovered in 1861 by German chemists Robert Bunsen and Gustav Kirchhoff. They identified it using a spectroscope, observing two distinct red lines in the spectrum of a mineral called lepidolite. They named it "rubidium" from the Latin word "rubidus," meaning "deepest red," referring to these spectral lines.

Abundance & Sources

Rubidium is the 16th most abundant element in the Earth's crust, making it more common than copper. However, it is widely dispersed and rarely found in concentrated deposits. It is often found in small amounts in minerals like lepidolite, pollucite, and carnallite.

Industrial Preparation

Rubidium is primarily obtained as a byproduct of lithium production or from the processing of other alkali metal minerals. It is extracted by various methods, including fractional crystallization of salts or by reduction of rubidium compounds with calcium or magnesium.

Uses

  • Used in atomic clocks, providing highly accurate timekeeping.
  • In high-tech vacuum tubes and photocells, due to its ability to be easily ionized.
  • Used in some specialized glasses and ceramics.
  • Rubidium carbonate is used in the production of some optical fibers.
  • Rubidium-87 is a radioactive isotope used in radiometric dating of rocks and minerals.

Interesting Facts

  • Rubidium is a highly reactive alkali metal and ignites spontaneously in air, forming rubidium oxides.
  • It reacts violently with water, producing hydrogen gas and a significant amount of heat.
  • It is one of the few elements that are liquid near room temperature, though its melting point is slightly higher than gallium.
  • Its flame test produces a distinctive purplish-red color.

Did You Know?

Due to its strong photoemission properties, rubidium is used in "atomic clocks" which are among the most accurate timekeeping devices, losing only about one second in many millions of years. This precision is vital for technologies like GPS.
