Ruthenium - Element Details

Symbol: Ru

Atomic Number: 44

Atomic Mass: 101.07 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d⁷ 5s¹

Group: 8 (Transition Metals)

Period: 5

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal, Platinum Group Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, brittle metal

Density: ~12.45 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~2334 °C

Boiling Point: ~4150 °C

Discovery

Ruthenium was discovered in 1844 by Russian chemist Karl Ernst Claus at Kazan State University. He isolated the new element from the crude platinum ore that came from the Ural Mountains. He named it "ruthenium" in honor of Russia, from the Latin word "Ruthenia."

Abundance & Sources

Ruthenium is a very rare element and is typically found as a minor component in platinum group metal ores, particularly in the Ural Mountains and in deposits in North and South America. It is mainly a byproduct of the mining of nickel and copper.

Industrial Preparation

The extraction of ruthenium is a complex process due to its low concentration in ores and its chemical similarity to other platinum group metals. It is separated from the other metals as a byproduct of nickel, copper, and platinum refining, often involving a series of chemical treatments and distillation.

Uses

  • Used as a hardener for platinum and palladium alloys to create wear-resistant electrical contacts.
  • In the form of ruthenium dioxide, it is a versatile catalyst in the chemical industry.
  • A small amount of ruthenium added to titanium can dramatically increase its corrosion resistance.
  • Used in some specialized applications such as pen nibs, and in a new application as the capping layer for extreme ultraviolet photomasks.
  • Some ruthenium complexes are being researched for use in solar energy and as potential anti-cancer drugs.

Interesting Facts

  • Ruthenium is one of the platinum group metals and is known for its extreme hardness and brittleness.
  • It is resistant to most chemical attacks, including all acids.
  • Ruthenium can exhibit a wide range of oxidation states, from +8 to -2.

Did You Know?

Adding a tiny amount of ruthenium to titanium can increase its corrosion resistance by a factor of a hundred. This property makes it invaluable for certain industrial applications where titanium's durability needs to be maximized.
