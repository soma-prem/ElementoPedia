Rutherfordium - Element Details

Symbol: Rf

Atomic Number: 104

Atomic Mass: [267] u

Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f¹⁴ 6d² 7s²

Group: 4 (Transition Metals)

Period: 7

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Unknown, likely silvery-white

Density: ~23.2 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~2100 °C

Boiling Point: ~5500 °C

Discovery

Rutherfordium was discovered in 1964 by a team of Soviet scientists at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research in Dubna, Russia, and independently in 1969 by a team led by **Albert Ghiorso** at the University of California, Berkeley. There was a naming dispute between the two groups. The name **Rutherfordium** was eventually adopted by the IUPAC in honor of **Ernest Rutherford**, the "father" of nuclear physics.

Abundance & Sources

Rutherfordium is a synthetic, radioactive element that does not exist in nature. It is produced in laboratories in minute quantities for scientific research.

Industrial Preparation

Rutherfordium is produced by nuclear fusion reactions in particle accelerators. For example, by bombarding a target of Californium-249 with Carbon-12 ions. The resulting product is a new, heavier element. Only a few atoms are produced at a time.

Uses

  • Rutherfordium has **no commercial applications** due to its extreme scarcity, high radioactivity, and very short half-life.
  • Its sole use is in **scientific research** to understand the fundamental properties of the heaviest elements and to test theories of nuclear and atomic structure.

Interesting Facts

  • Rutherfordium is the **first transactinide element**, marking the beginning of the d-block in the seventh period of the periodic table.
  • The most stable isotope, Rutherfordium-267, has a half-life of about 1.3 hours.
  • It is an extremely heavy element, with an estimated density higher than that of gold or platinum.

Did You Know?

The naming dispute over rutherfordium was a significant event in the history of element discovery. The Soviet team proposed "Kurchatovium" after Igor Kurchatov, while the American team proposed "Rutherfordium." The IUPAC's final decision resolved the controversy.
