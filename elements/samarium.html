Samarium - Element Details

Symbol: Sm

Atomic Number: 62

Atomic Mass: 150.36 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f⁶ 6s²

Group: 3 (Lanthanides)

Period: 6

Block: f-block

Category: Lanthanide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, lustrous metal

Density: ~7.52 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1072 °C

Boiling Point: ~1794 °C

Discovery

Samarium was discovered in 1879 by French chemist **Paul-Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran**. While examining the mineral samarskite, he found new spectral lines that indicated the presence of a new element. He named the element Samarium after the mineral, which itself was named in honor of **Vasili Samarsky-Bykhovets**, a Russian mining engineer.

Abundance & Sources

Samarium is a moderately abundant rare-earth element, with concentrations in the Earth's crust comparable to tin. It is never found in its pure form in nature. The primary commercial sources are the minerals monazite, bastnäsite, and samarskite, with major deposits found in China, the United States, and Australia.

Industrial Preparation

The industrial production of samarium involves a series of complex separation steps to isolate it from other rare-earth elements in the ore. The separation is primarily done using solvent extraction or ion-exchange chromatography. The pure metal is then produced by reducing samarium oxide ($Sm_2O_3$) with a more reactive metal, such as lanthanum, in a vacuum at high temperatures.

Uses

  • The most significant use of samarium is in the production of **samarium-cobalt (SmCo) magnets**, which are the second strongest type of rare-earth permanent magnets.
  • SmCo magnets are highly resistant to demagnetization and can operate at high temperatures (up to 700°C), making them essential for military technology, aerospace, and high-performance motors.
  • Samarium-149 is a crucial component in **nuclear reactor control rods** due to its very high capacity to absorb neutrons.
  • It is also used to dope calcium fluoride crystals for optoelectronic applications.
  • Samarium compounds are used in infrared-absorbing glass and in some phosphors.

Interesting Facts

  • Samarium is a hard, brittle, and silvery-white metal that tarnishes slowly in air.
  • Samarium has a high neutron absorption cross-section, which is a key reason for its use in nuclear reactors.
  • The discovery of the mineral samarskite, and subsequently the element samarium, marked the beginning of the age of rare-earth element discoveries.

Did You Know?

Samarium-cobalt magnets retain their magnetic properties at much higher temperatures than neodymium magnets, making them invaluable for applications like high-performance motors in aerospace or defense systems where operating temperatures are a concern.
