Seaborgium - Element Details

Symbol: Sg

Group: 6 (Transition Metals)

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Unknown, likely silvery-white or gray

Boiling Point: ~- °C

Discovery

Seaborgium was discovered in 1974 by a team of scientists led by **Albert Ghiorso** at the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory in California. They synthesized it by bombarding a californium-249 target with oxygen-18 ions in a particle accelerator. The element was named in honor of the American nuclear chemist **Glenn T. Seaborg**, a pioneer in the discovery of many transuranic elements.

Abundance & Sources

Seaborgium is a synthetic, radioactive element that does not exist in nature. It is produced in laboratories in minute quantities for scientific research.

Industrial Preparation

Seaborgium is produced by nuclear fusion reactions in particle accelerators. One method involves bombarding a target of Californium-249 with a beam of Oxygen-18 ions. The resulting product is a new, heavier element.

Uses

  • Seaborgium has **no commercial applications** due to its extreme scarcity, high radioactivity, and very short half-life.
  • Its sole use is in **scientific research** to further understand the fundamental properties of the heaviest elements.

Interesting Facts

  • Seaborgium is a highly radioactive element with a very short half-life. The most stable isotope, Seaborgium-271, has a half-life of only about 2.4 minutes.
  • It is the first element to be named after a living person. The naming of the element sparked controversy, but the IUPAC ultimately made an exception to its policy and honored Glenn Seaborg.
  • Seaborgium is a very dense element, with an estimated density of about 35 g/cm³.

Did You Know?

The naming of seaborgium was a significant moment in the history of science. Glenn T. Seaborg was a lead scientist on the discovery team and was still alive at the time the element was named, a rare honor that highlighted his monumental contributions to nuclear chemistry.
