Selenium - Element Details

Symbol: Se

Atomic Number: 34

Atomic Mass: 78.971 u

Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s² 4p⁴

Group: 16 (Chalcogens)

Period: 4

Block: p-block

Category: Nonmetal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Red (amorphous), grey (metallic)

Density: ~4.81 g/cm³ (grey Se)

Melting Point: 221 °C (grey Se)

Boiling Point: 685 °C

Discovery

Selenium was discovered in 1817 by Swedish chemist Jöns Jacob Berzelius, who named it after Selene, the Greek goddess of the Moon, due to its chemical similarity to tellurium (named after Tellus, the Earth).

Abundance & Sources

Selenium is a relatively rare element in the Earth's crust. It is often found as a minor component in sulfide ores of copper, lead, and nickel. It is typically recovered as a byproduct of copper refining.

Industrial Preparation

Most commercial selenium is produced as a byproduct from the anode slimes of electrolytic copper refineries. The slimes are treated to recover selenium, often involving roasting with soda ash or sulfuric acid to form soluble selenium compounds, followed by reduction to elemental selenium.

Uses

  • Used in glass manufacturing to decolorize glass or to impart a red color to glass and enamels.
  • Historically used in photocells and solar cells due to its photoconductive properties (its electrical conductivity increases with light exposure).
  • Important component in rectifiers and other electronic devices.
  • Used as an additive in stainless steel to improve machinability.
  • Essential trace element for human and animal health, crucial for antioxidant enzymes and thyroid hormone metabolism.
  • Selenium sulfide is an active ingredient in some anti-dandruff shampoos.

Interesting Facts

  • Selenium exhibits photoconductivity, meaning its electrical conductivity increases when exposed to light, a property that led to its early use in light meters and solar cells.
  • It can exist in several allotropic forms with different physical properties, including a red amorphous form and a gray metallic form.
  • Both too little and too much selenium can be detrimental to health.

Did You Know?

The ability of selenium to convert light into electricity was crucial for the early development of "electric eyes" and played a role in the nascent field of television technology in the early 20th century.
