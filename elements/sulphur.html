Sulfur – Element Details

Symbol: S

Atomic Number: 16

Atomic Mass: 32.06 u

Electron Configuration: [Ne] 3s2 3p4

Group: 16 (Chalcogens)

Period: 3

Category: Non‑metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Pale yellow crystals or powder

Melting Point: ~115.2 °C

Boiling Point: ~444.6 °C

Density: ≈ 2.07 g/cm³

Discovery

Sulfur has been known since ancient times (Egypt, Greece). In 1777, Lavoisier established it was a chemical element. Pure sulfur isolated by Gay‑Lussac and Thénard in early 1800s. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

Abundance & Sources

Occurs naturally in elemental form near volcanic regions and hot springs; common in sulfide and sulfate minerals. Global production is mostly a byproduct from oil and gas refining. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

Uses

  • Main feedstock for sulfuric acid – essential in fertilizer production
  • Used in matches, gunpowder (black powder), vulcanization of rubber
  • Used as fungicides, insecticides, and in bleaching/paper industry

Interesting Facts

  • Sulfur compounds cause the distinct odor in rotten eggs, skunk, grapefruit, natural gas (e.g., H₂S) :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}
  • It is essential for life—present in amino acids (cysteine, methionine), vitamins, and iron‑sulfur enzymes :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}
  • Bright yellow crystals; poor conductor of electricity; insoluble in water

Did You Know?

The Biblical term “brimstone” refers to sulfur. Ancient Egyptians used it in ointments over 4,000 years ago. Sulfuric acid remains one of the most widely produced industrial chemicals. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}
