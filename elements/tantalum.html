Tantalum - Element Details

Symbol: Ta

Atomic Number: 73

Atomic Mass: 180.94788 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d³ 6s²

Group: 5 (Transition Metals)

Period: 6

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Gray, heavy, and very hard metal

Density: ~16.69 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~3017 °C

Boiling Point: ~5458 °C

Discovery

Tantalum was discovered in 1802 by Swedish chemist **Anders Ekeberg**. He isolated it from a mineral sample found in Sweden. He named the element after **Tantalus**, a figure from Greek mythology who was condemned to stand in water and under a fruit tree, but could not reach either. This name was a reference to the element's "tantalizing" and frustrating difficulty in dissolving in acids.

Abundance & Sources

Tantalum is a rare element. It is almost always found in nature together with niobium in the mineral **tantalite**. Tantalite and its counterpart columbite form a mineral series known as **coltan**. Major deposits are found in the Democratic Republic of Congo, Brazil, Canada, and Australia.

Industrial Preparation

The extraction of tantalum from coltan is a complex process. The ore is first treated with a mixture of hydrofluoric acid and sulfuric acid to dissolve the metals. Tantalum is then separated from niobium using solvent extraction. The purified tantalum compound is then reduced with sodium or carbon to produce the pure tantalum metal.

Uses

  • The primary use of tantalum is in the production of **capacitors** for electronics. Tantalum capacitors are known for their small size, high capacitance, and stability, making them vital for mobile phones, laptops, and digital cameras.
  • Tantalum is used in **high-temperature alloys** for aircraft jet engines, nuclear reactors, and missile parts due to its high melting point and resistance to corrosion.
  • Because it is non-irritating and non-reactive with body tissues, it is used in **surgical implants**, such as bone plates and screws.
  • It is also used in laboratory equipment and in chemical processing applications where strong acids are present.

Interesting Facts

  • Tantalum is highly ductile and can be drawn into a fine wire. It is also extremely hard, making it a difficult material to work with.
  • The melting point of tantalum is the third highest of all elements, exceeded only by tungsten and rhenium.
  • Tantalum forms a passive oxide layer on its surface, which provides it with excellent resistance to corrosion.

Did You Know?

The name "coltan" is a portmanteau of the two minerals that make up the ore, columbite and tantalite. The high demand for tantalum in consumer electronics has made coltan mining a significant, and sometimes controversial, industry in parts of the world with large deposits.
