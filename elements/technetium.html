Technetium - Element Details

Symbol: Tc

Atomic Number: 43

Atomic Mass: 98 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d⁵ 5s²

Group: 7 (Transition Metals)

Period: 5

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal, Radioactive

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-gray, metallic

Density: ~11.5 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~2172 °C

Boiling Point: ~4877 °C

Discovery

Technetium was the first element to be artificially produced. It was discovered in 1937 by Italian-American physicists Carlo Perrier and Emilio Segrè. They analyzed a sample of molybdenum that had been bombarded with deuterons in a cyclotron at the University of California, Berkeley. Its name comes from the Greek word "technetos," meaning "artificial."

Abundance & Sources

Technetium is virtually absent from the Earth's crust as a primordial element due to the short half-lives of its isotopes. It is, however, found in trace amounts from the spontaneous fission of uranium. The vast majority of technetium is produced artificially in nuclear reactors as a fission product.

Industrial Preparation

The most abundant isotope, Technetium-99, is a byproduct of uranium fission in nuclear reactors. It is extracted from the waste products of nuclear fuel rods, which contain a mixture of fission products. The metastable isotope technetium-99m is produced from the decay of molybdenum-99 in specialized generators for medical use.

Uses

  • The radioisotope Technetium-99m is the most widely used medical radioisotope for diagnostic imaging, including bone scans and heart function tests.
  • Technetium compounds are used in corrosion-resistant products and as a metallurgical tracer.
  • Technetium metal is a superconductor at temperatures below 11.2 K.
  • It is also found in some S-type stars, which has provided valuable insights into stellar nucleosynthesis.

Interesting Facts

  • Technetium is the lightest element with no stable isotopes. All of its isotopes are radioactive.
  • It has a chemical behavior similar to that of rhenium and manganese.
  • Technetium's presence in stars was a key piece of evidence that elements are formed in stars.

Did You Know?

The primary use of Technetium-99m in medicine is due to its ideal properties for diagnostic imaging: its short half-life of 6 hours allows for a quick scan with minimal radiation exposure, and it emits gamma rays that are easily detected by medical cameras.
