Tennessine - Element Details

Symbol: Ts

Atomic Number: 117

Atomic Mass: [294] u

Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f¹⁴ 6d¹⁰ 7s² 7p⁵

Group: 17 (Halogens)

Period: 7

Block: p-block

Category: Nonmetal (predicted)

State at Room Temperature: Solid (predicted)

Color: Unknown, but likely a metallic appearance

Density: ~7.2 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~350 - 550 °C

Boiling Point: ~610 °C

Discovery

Tennessine was discovered in 2010 by a Russian-American team at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research (JINR) in Dubna, Russia. The team included scientists from the Oak Ridge National Laboratory and Vanderbilt University. The element was named in honor of the U.S. state of **Tennessee**, home to the Oak Ridge National Laboratory and Vanderbilt University, which were key partners in the discovery.

Abundance & Sources

Tennessine is a synthetic, radioactive element that does not exist in nature. It is produced in laboratories in minute quantities for scientific research.

Industrial Preparation

Tennessine is produced by nuclear fusion reactions in a particle accelerator. A target of Berkelium-249 is bombarded with a beam of Calcium-48 ions, causing the nuclei to fuse and create an atom of tennessine.

Uses

  • Tennessine has **no commercial applications** due to its extreme scarcity, high radioactivity, and very short half-life.
  • Its sole use is in **scientific research** to further understand the fundamental properties of the heaviest elements.

Interesting Facts

  • Tennessine is a highly radioactive element with a very short half-life. The most stable isotope, Tennessine-294, has a half-life of only about 51 milliseconds.
  • It is the second-to-last element on the periodic table and is a halogen, but due to relativistic effects, its chemical properties are expected to be significantly different from other halogens.
  • The discovery of tennessine required a collaborative effort between laboratories in Russia and the United States, highlighting the importance of international cooperation in scientific research.

Did You Know?

The discovery of tennessine, along with other superheavy elements, provided crucial evidence for the existence of the theorized "**island of stability**," an area where certain superheavy elements might have longer half-lives than predicted.
