Terbium - Element Details

Symbol: Tb

Atomic Number: 65

Atomic Mass: 158.925 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f⁹ 6s²

Group: 3 (Lanthanides)

Period: 6

Block: f-block

Category: Lanthanide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, malleable, and ductile metal

Density: ~8.23 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1356 °C

Boiling Point: ~3230 °C

Discovery

Terbium was discovered in 1843 by Swedish chemist **Carl Gustaf Mosander**. He found the element as a new impurity in yttrium oxide, which he was studying from a mineral found at the Ytterby mine in Sweden. He named the element "terbium" after the village of Ytterby, which has given its name to four elements (Yttrium, Ytterbium, Terbium, and Erbium).

Abundance & Sources

Terbium is a rare-earth element that is not found in its pure form in nature. It is typically found in rare-earth minerals such as monazite and xenotime, alongside other lanthanides. The primary sources for these minerals are located in China, the United States, and Australia.

Industrial Preparation

The industrial production of terbium involves a series of complex separation steps to isolate it from the other rare-earth elements in the ore. The separation is primarily achieved using solvent extraction or ion-exchange chromatography. The pure metal is then produced by the reduction of anhydrous terbium fluoride ($TbF_3$) with calcium metal at high temperatures.

Uses

  • Terbium is a key component in **green phosphors** used in fluorescent lamps, color TV screens, and LED lighting. Its compounds emit a bright green light when excited.
  • It is a crucial component in the alloy **Terfenol-D** (Terbium, Dysprosium, Iron), which exhibits magnetostriction. This property is used in actuators, sonar systems, and sensors.
  • Terbium is used as a dopant in solid-state devices and in certain types of fuel cells.
  • Its unique luminescent properties make it valuable in **anti-counterfeiting** measures, as it can be used in phosphors that glow under UV light.

Interesting Facts

  • Terbium is a soft, malleable, and ductile metal that is stable in air, forming a protective oxide layer.
  • It exhibits a strong green fluorescence when exposed to ultraviolet light, a property that is central to many of its applications.
  • Terbium is a component of Terfenol-D, an alloy that can change its shape with a change in a magnetic field, a property that makes it useful in devices like high-performance speakers.

Did You Know?

The green color produced by terbium phosphors is one of the three primary colors (red, green, and blue) needed to create a full spectrum of colors on a television or computer screen.
