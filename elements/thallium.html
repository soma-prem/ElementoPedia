Thallium - Element Details

Symbol: Tl

Atomic Number: 81

Atomic Mass: 204.3833 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s² 6p¹

Group: 13 (Post-transition Metals)

Period: 6

Block: p-block

Category: Poor Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Bluish-white, soft, malleable metal

Density: ~11.85 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~304 °C

Boiling Point: ~1473 °C

Discovery

Thallium was discovered in 1861 by English chemist **Sir William Crookes**. He was using a spectroscope to analyze the residues left over from sulfuric acid production and noticed a new, bright green spectral line. He named the element "thallium" from the Greek word *thallos*, meaning "**green twig**," in honor of this distinctive line. The metal itself was isolated a year later, independently by both Crookes and French chemist Claude-Auguste Lamy.

Abundance & Sources

Thallium is a relatively rare element. It is not found in its pure form in nature but is found in trace amounts in minerals containing potassium. It is primarily obtained as a minor byproduct of refining sulfide ores, particularly those of zinc and lead.

Industrial Preparation

Industrial production of thallium is almost entirely a recovery process from the flue dust of smelters that process sulfide ores. The thallium-containing dust is treated with sulfuric acid to dissolve the thallium, which is then purified through electrolysis or chemical precipitation to produce the pure metal.

Uses

  • Historically, thallium compounds were widely used as **rat poisons and insecticides** due to their effectiveness and lack of color or smell. However, this use has been banned in many countries because of the risk to human health.
  • In the field of optics, thallium is used in some **photoelectric cells** and in certain types of glass with a high refractive index.
  • The radioisotope **Thallium-201** is used in nuclear medicine for heart stress tests, to visualize blood flow to the heart.
  • Thallium compounds have been investigated for use in infrared detectors and superconductors.

Interesting Facts

  • Thallium is a **highly toxic heavy metal**. Its compounds are often tasteless and odorless, which made them a favored tool for poisoning in literature and criminal history.
  • It is a very soft and malleable metal, so soft that it can be cut with a knife. When first exposed to air, it has a bluish-white luster, but this quickly tarnishes to a grayish color.
  • One of the most famous symptoms of thallium poisoning is hair loss, which can be mistaken for other medical conditions.

Did You Know?

Due to the extreme toxicity of thallium, its use in consumer products has been almost completely phased out. However, its radioisotope, Thallium-201, is a safe and invaluable tool in medical diagnostics, highlighting the distinction between an element's toxic properties and its useful nuclear properties.
