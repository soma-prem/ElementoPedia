Thorium - Element Details

Symbol: Th

Atomic Number: 90

Atomic Mass: 232.0377 u

Electron Configuration: [Rn] 6d² 7s²

Group: 3 (Actinides)

Period: 7

Block: d-block

Category: Actinide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, tarnishes to black

Density: ~11.72 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1750 °C

Boiling Point: ~4788 °C

Discovery

Thorium was discovered in 1828 by Swedish chemist **Jöns Jacob Berzelius**. He isolated the new element from a mineral sample found on an island off the coast of Norway. Berzelius named the element after **Thor**, the Norse god of thunder, reflecting its discovery in a Scandinavian region.

Abundance & Sources

Thorium is a naturally occurring radioactive element. It is more abundant than uranium, found in concentrations comparable to lead. The primary commercial source of thorium is **monazite sand**, which is a heavy mineral found in beach sands in countries like India, Brazil, and Australia.

Industrial Preparation

The industrial preparation of thorium involves several steps. The monazite sand is first crushed and then treated with strong acids or alkalis to dissolve the thorium and other rare earth elements. The thorium compounds are then separated from the other elements, and the metal is finally produced by reducing thorium oxide ($ThO_2$) with calcium or by electrolysis.

Uses

  • Thorium is being actively explored as a **nuclear fuel** for next-generation nuclear reactors. Thorium-based reactors are a potential alternative to uranium reactors as they are more efficient and produce less long-lived radioactive waste.
  • Historically, thorium dioxide was a key component in **gas lantern mantles**, where it produced a bright, white light when heated. This use has largely been phased out due to its radioactivity.
  • An alloy of thorium and magnesium is used to improve the strength and high-temperature resistance of aerospace components.
  • Thorium is also used as an alloying agent in welding electrodes and in high-quality optical glass.

Interesting Facts

  • Thorium is radioactive, but its most common isotope, Thorium-232, has a half-life of over 14 billion years, making it a primordial element that has existed since the formation of the Earth.
  • It is a soft, silvery-white metal that tarnishes slowly in air, turning a gray or black color.
  • Thorium is the first element in the actinide series with a filling f-orbital, although its ground-state electron configuration is an exception to the general trend.

Did You Know?

Thorium-based nuclear power plants have several advantages, including the potential for a more abundant fuel source and the production of less plutonium waste. This has led to renewed interest in thorium as a cleaner and safer long-term energy source.
