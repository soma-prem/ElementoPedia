Thulium - Element Details

Symbol: Tm

Atomic Number: 69

Atomic Mass: 168.934 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f¹³ 6s²

Group: 3 (Lanthanides)

Period: 6

Block: f-block

Category: Lanthanide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-gray, lustrous metal

Density: ~9.32 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1545 °C

Boiling Point: ~1950 °C

Discovery

Thulium was discovered in 1879 by Swedish chemist **Per Teodor Cleve**. While working with a sample of the mineral erbia (the oxide of Erbium), he was able to separate a new, brown substance which he named "thulia." He later identified this as the oxide of a new element. He named it "thulium" after **Thule**, an ancient name for Scandinavia, the region where the element was discovered.

Abundance & Sources

Thulium is one of the rarest lanthanide elements and is never found in its pure form in nature. It is typically found in very small quantities in rare-earth minerals such as monazite and xenotime, alongside other lanthanides. The primary sources for these minerals are located in China, the United States, and Australia.

Industrial Preparation

The industrial production of thulium is a complex and challenging process due to its rarity. It is extracted from rare-earth minerals, often as a byproduct of erbium or ytterbium production, using a series of solvent extraction and ion-exchange chromatography steps. The pure metal is then produced by the reduction of anhydrous thulium fluoride ($TmF_3$) with calcium metal at high temperatures.

Uses

  • Thulium is used as the active medium in certain **lasers** for medical and industrial applications, particularly for surgery and cutting.
  • The radioactive isotope **Thulium-170** is used in portable X-ray devices for non-destructive testing and in medical diagnostics, as it does not require a large, external power source.
  • It is also used as a component in some magnetic ceramics and alloys.
  • Thulium has been investigated for use in military and meteorological laser applications.

Interesting Facts

  • Thulium is the second-least abundant of the lanthanide elements in the Earth's crust, with only the radioactive promethium being rarer.
  • It is a fairly soft, silvery-gray metal that is malleable and ductile.
  • Thulium's name is a poetic tribute to the discovery location, as "Thule" was a mythical place believed to be at the edge of the world.

Did You Know?

The use of Thulium-170 in portable X-ray machines has revolutionized diagnostics in remote or emergency settings where traditional, heavy X-ray equipment is not feasible. This isotope provides a compact and reliable source of radiation.
