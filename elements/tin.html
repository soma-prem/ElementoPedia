Tin - Element Details

Symbol: Sn

Atomic Number: 50

Atomic Mass: 118.710 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d¹⁰ 5s² 5p²

Group: 14 (Post-transition Metals)

Period: 5

Block: p-block

Category: Poor Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, malleable metal

Density: ~7.31 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~231.93 °C

Boiling Point: ~2602 °C

Discovery

Tin has been known since ancient times and is one of the oldest metals used by humans. Its discovery is not attributed to a single person. It was a key component in the production of bronze, an alloy of copper and tin, which marked the beginning of the Bronze Age around 3000 BC.

Abundance & Sources

Tin is a relatively rare element in the Earth's crust. It is primarily obtained from the mineral cassiterite (tin oxide, $SnO_2$). Major producers of tin ore are China, Indonesia, Peru, and Brazil.

Industrial Preparation

The extraction of tin from its ore, cassiterite, is a straightforward process. The ore is first crushed and then washed to remove impurities. It is then roasted to remove sulfur and arsenic, followed by reduction in a furnace with carbon to produce molten tin. This crude tin is then refined by heating it to a low temperature where the pure tin melts and flows away, leaving behind impurities.

Uses

  • Tin is most commonly used as a coating for other metals, such as steel, to prevent corrosion (e.g., in tin cans).
  • It is a key component in a variety of alloys, most notably bronze (tin and copper) and solder (tin and lead, or lead-free alternatives).
  • Tin oxide is used in ceramics and glazes to produce a white, opaque finish.
  • It is used in glass manufacturing to create float glass, where molten glass floats on a bed of molten tin to produce a perfectly flat surface.
  • Tin is also used in the production of organ pipes, bells, and other musical instruments.

Interesting Facts

  • Tin has a distinctive "tin cry" or "tin-plague" when it is bent. This is the sound of the metal's crystal structure twinning.
  • Pure tin can change its form (allotrope) at low temperatures. "White tin" (beta tin) is metallic, but below 13.2 °C, it slowly converts to "grey tin" (alpha tin), a non-metallic, brittle powder. This phenomenon is known as "tin pest."
  • The chemical symbol for tin, Sn, comes from the Latin word "stannum."

Did You Know?

The use of tin in solder has changed significantly over the years. Due to health concerns about lead, lead-free solders, which are primarily tin-based alloys, are now standard in most electronics manufacturing.
