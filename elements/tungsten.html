Tungsten - Element Details

Symbol: W

Atomic Number: 74

Atomic Mass: 183.84 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d⁴ 6s²

Group: 6 (Transition Metals)

Period: 6

Block: d-block

Category: Transition Metal

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white to gray metal

Density: ~19.25 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~3422 °C

Boiling Point: ~5930 °C

Discovery

Tungsten was first identified as an element in 1781 by Swedish chemist **Carl Wilhelm Scheele**. He discovered a new acid, tungstic acid, in the mineral scheelite. However, it was not isolated as a pure metal until 1783, when Spanish chemists **Fausto and Juan José Elhuyar** successfully reduced the acid from a different mineral, wolframite. They are credited with the discovery of the element.

Abundance & Sources

Tungsten is a rare element in the Earth's crust and is never found in its native form. The primary ores from which it is extracted are **wolframite** ($FeWO_4$) and **scheelite** ($CaWO_4$). China is by far the largest producer of tungsten, followed by Russia and Vietnam.

Industrial Preparation

The industrial process for obtaining tungsten involves several steps. The ore is first treated with an alkali to form tungsten trioxide ($WO_3$), which is then heated and reduced with hydrogen gas or carbon to produce pure tungsten powder. This powder is then pressed and sintered at high temperatures to form the solid, dense metal.

Uses

  • Tungsten is most famous for its use in the **filaments of incandescent light bulbs** due to its extremely high melting point.
  • Its hardness and density make it a key component in **high-speed steel** and other alloys used in cutting tools, armor-piercing ammunition, and heavy-duty machinery.
  • **Tungsten carbide** is one of the hardest materials known to man and is used extensively in drilling bits, milling tools, and armor plates.
  • It is used in certain types of electrodes for welding, in electrical contacts, and in heating elements for high-temperature furnaces.

Interesting Facts

  • Tungsten has the **highest melting point of all metals** (3422 °C) and the highest boiling point of any element (5930 °C).
  • Its name comes from the Swedish words *tung sten*, meaning "**heavy stone**." The element's chemical symbol, W, is derived from its German name, wolfram.
  • The filament in a standard incandescent light bulb is a tiny, coiled tungsten wire that can reach temperatures of over 2000 °C, glowing white-hot without melting.

Did You Know?

Because of its extreme hardness and durability, tungsten carbide has become a popular material for high-end jewelry, particularly wedding rings, which are highly scratch-resistant and maintain their polish for a lifetime.
