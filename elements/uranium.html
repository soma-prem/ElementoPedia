Uranium - Element Details

Symbol: U

Atomic Number: 92

Atomic Mass: 238.02891 u

Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f³ 6d¹ 7s²

Group: 3 (Actinides)

Period: 7

Block: f-block

Category: Actinide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, heavy, radioactive metal

Density: ~19.1 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~1132.2 °C

Boiling Point: ~4131 °C

Discovery

Uranium was discovered in 1789 by German chemist **Martin Heinrich Klaproth**. He was analyzing a sample of the mineral pitchblende and isolated a new element which he named "uranium" after the planet Uranus, which had been discovered just eight years earlier. The metal itself was not isolated until 1841 by French chemist Eugène-Melchior Péligot.

Abundance & Sources

Uranium is a common element in the Earth's crust, found in concentrations higher than silver. It is never found in its native, pure form. The primary commercial source of uranium is uranium-rich minerals like **uraninite** (also known as pitchblende). Major producers of uranium ore are Kazakhstan, Canada, and Australia.

Industrial Preparation

Uranium ore is mined, crushed, and then leached with sulfuric acid or an alkaline solution to dissolve the uranium. The uranium is then purified through various chemical processes and precipitated to form a powder known as **"yellowcake"**. This yellowcake is then converted into uranium dioxide or uranium hexafluoride for use in nuclear reactors.

Uses

  • The primary use of uranium is as a **nuclear fuel** in nuclear power plants to generate electricity.
  • Uranium, specifically highly enriched uranium, is used in **nuclear weapons**.
  • Due to its very high density, depleted uranium (mostly U-238) is used in **kinetic energy penetrators** for military ammunition and as counterweights in aircraft.
  • It is also used for shielding against radiation and in some specialized chemical catalysts.

Interesting Facts

  • Uranium is a highly radioactive element, but its most common isotope, Uranium-238, has a half-life of over 4.5 billion years, which is roughly the age of the Earth.
  • The fission of one kilogram of uranium-235 (a fissile isotope) can produce as much energy as 3 million tons of coal.
  • Uranium is extremely dense, about 70% denser than lead and only slightly less dense than gold or tungsten.

Did You Know?

The discovery of uranium's ability to undergo nuclear fission in the late 1930s led directly to the development of both nuclear power and nuclear weapons, fundamentally changing the course of the 20th century and inaugurating the nuclear age.
