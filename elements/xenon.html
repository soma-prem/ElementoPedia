Xenon - Element Details

Symbol: Xe

Atomic Number: 54

Atomic Mass: 131.293 u

Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d¹⁰ 5s² 5p⁶

Group: 18 (Noble Gases)

Period: 5

Block: p-block

Category: Noble Gas

State at Room Temperature: Gas

Color: Colorless gas; blue glow in an electric field

Density: ~5.894 g/L

Melting Point: -111.7 °C

Boiling Point: -108.1 °C

Discovery

Xenon was discovered in 1898 by Scottish chemist William Ramsay and English chemist Morris Travers in London. They discovered the element in the residue left over after evaporating liquid air. They named it "xenon" after the Greek word "xenos," meaning "stranger" or "foreign," to reflect its rarity.

Abundance & Sources

Xenon is a trace element in the Earth's atmosphere, present in about 1 part per 11.5 million. It is found in the atmosphere and is extracted commercially by fractional distillation of liquid air, a process that also produces krypton and neon.

Industrial Preparation

Xenon is primarily obtained as a byproduct of the large-scale production of liquid oxygen and liquid nitrogen through the fractional distillation of liquid air. This process involves cooling air to extremely low temperatures until it liquefies, then slowly heating it to separate the components based on their different boiling points. Xenon is one of the last gases to be produced in this process.

Uses

  • Xenon is used in high-intensity gas-discharge lamps, such as those used in cinematic projectors and high-performance car headlights (HID or Xenon headlights).
  • It is used in flash lamps for photography, stroboscopic lamps, and lasers.
  • In medicine, xenon is used as a general anesthetic and in medical imaging, particularly in MRI scans.
  • Xenon is used in ion propulsion systems for satellites and other spacecraft.
  • It is also used in some nuclear energy applications, as it can absorb neutrons.

Interesting Facts

  • Xenon is a noble gas, and for a long time, it was believed to be completely inert. However, in 1962, the first noble gas compound, xenon hexafluoroplatinate, was synthesized.
  • Xenon has a full outer electron shell, which makes it very stable and unreactive.
  • The beautiful blue glow in a xenon lamp is due to the gas becoming ionized and emitting light.

Did You Know?

The use of xenon in ion propulsion, for example on the Dawn spacecraft, is highly efficient. The propellant is ionized and accelerated by an electric field, providing a small but continuous thrust that can propel a spacecraft for many years.
