Ytterbium - Element Details

Symbol: Yb

Atomic Number: 70

Atomic Mass: 173.045 u

Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 6s²

Group: 3 (Lanthanides)

Period: 6

Block: f-block

Category: Lanthanide

State at Room Temperature: Solid

Color: Silvery-white, malleable and ductile metal

Density: ~6.96 g/cm³

Melting Point: ~819 °C

Boiling Point: ~1196 °C

Discovery

Ytterbium was discovered in 1878 by Swiss chemist **Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac**. He was analyzing the mineral known as erbia (the oxide of erbium), and he found new, distinct spectral lines. He correctly identified this as a new element, which he named "ytterbium." Like three other elements, it was named after the village of **Ytterby**, Sweden, where the mineral was first discovered.

Abundance & Sources

Ytterbium is a moderately abundant rare-earth element, though never found in its pure form in nature. It is typically found in rare-earth minerals such as monazite and xenotime. The primary sources for these minerals are located in China, the United States, and Australia.

Industrial Preparation

The industrial production of ytterbium involves a series of complex separation steps to isolate it from the other rare-earth elements in the ore. The separation is primarily achieved using solvent extraction and ion-exchange chromatography. The pure metal is then produced by the reduction of anhydrous ytterbium fluoride ($YbF_3$) with calcium metal at high temperatures.

Uses

  • Ytterbium is used as a dopant in **fiber optic cables** for signal amplification, particularly in high-power fiber lasers.
  • The metal is used in **certain high-pressure applications**, including in some gauges and strain sensors because of its unusual electrical resistance properties under stress.
  • Its radioactive isotope, **Ytterbium-169**, is used as a portable X-ray source in medical imaging and industrial radiography.
  • Ytterbium is also used in some phosphors and in certain alloys to improve their mechanical properties.

Interesting Facts

  • Ytterbium has a relatively low density, melting point, and boiling point compared to other lanthanide elements.
  • The element is highly ductile and malleable, making it easy to work with in alloys.
  • Ytterbium has a full f-electron subshell, which is a key reason for its unique chemical and physical properties compared to its neighboring lanthanides.

Did You Know?

Ytterbium-doped fiber lasers are some of the most powerful and efficient lasers available today. They are used extensively in industrial manufacturing for precision cutting, welding, and marking, and are a core technology in modern production lines.
